<p>I found <a href="/2007/02/noah_lyon_at_ho_1.html">Noah Lyon</a> in the middle of the largest of the pieces in Jennifer Steinkamp's show, "<a href="http://www.artcal.net/event/view/6/7795">Daisy Bell</a>", last Sunday when Barry and I visited Lehmann Maupin's beautiful Lower East Side space.  Noah was covered with sections of Steinkamps' gently-waving projection of flowers as they paraded down the far wall of the darkened gallery on a black ground.</p>

<p>My thoughts about the show itself while I was in the gallery were something on the order of, "yes, it's beautiful, but (especially because of the extravagant high-tech element) so what?"  I didn't actually read the press release until much later (in fact only after looking on my computer screen at the picture above) and then it came together.  I have to remind myself that sometimes you shouldn't leave a show without looking at the "instructions".  Here's an excerpt from the gallery website, edited for some typos:</p>

<blockquote>
<p>The title, Daisy Bell, refers to a particular moment in the history of science and culture: when in 1962, Bell Labs used the IBM 704 to synthesize the popular 19th- Century English song of the same name.  The song was also used in the climactic scene of the epic film 2001: A Space Odyssey in which the supercomputer HAL 9000 begins to sing Daisy, Daisy as his consciousness is degraded.</p>

<p>Steinkamp's Daisy Bell series is comprised of a variety of poisonous flowers that appear to cascade down the gallery walls.</p>

<p>Much as Bell Lab's Daisy Bell consisted of a human application reinterpreting nature, Steinkamp reprises the idea, and defines this new series of artwork by its relationship to human innovations.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>The gallery's <a href="http://www.lehmannmaupin.com/#/exhibitions/2008-09-07_jennifer-steinkamp/">site</a> for this exhibition has more images, including a video of another piece being shown on Chrystie Street.</p>
