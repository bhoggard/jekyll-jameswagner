<p>In the last hours these two stories have appeared in the NYTimes:  <br />
<a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2006/06/24/washington/24swift.html">"Cheney Assails Press on Report on Bank Data"</a> and: <br />
<a href="http://www.nytimes.com/aponline/us/AP-Mosque-Raid-Secrecy.html?_r=1&oref=slogin">"Court Bars Info Request on NSA Wiretapping"</a></p>

<p>So, the engineer behind the systematic destruction of our liberties is outraged that the media might inform us of the fact, and in a related case our courts have once again ruled on the side of the rogue executive.  Even the third branch of our government is paralyzed to resist these authoritarian depredations, fearing the accusation of being soft on terrorism (the new McCarthyism) while ignoring the terrorism at the top.</p>

<p>Wednesday evening we were privileged to attend a magnificent performance of Heinar Kipphardt's 1968 <a href="http://theater2.nytimes.com/2006/06/13/theater/reviews/13opp.html?adxnnl=1&adxnnlx=1150951274-4X2uS19zhRFGTuWDvvDfJA">play</a>, "In the Matter of J. Robert Oppenheimer" at the Connelly Theatre in the East Village.   In the drama, which is based on actual transcripts from a 1954 hearing, Oppenhiemer has been summoned before a committee of the  Atomic Energy Commission charged with determining whether his security clearance will be reinstated.   In the first act he responds to one of the lawyers arguing against his case,</p>

<blockquote>
<p><strong>"There are people who are willing to protect freedom until there is nothing left of it"</strong>.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Can anyone say the phrase, "police state"?  Or are we going to wait until we are totally forbidden to do so?</p>
