<p>Count me in!</p>

<p>It's going to be a very interesting summer in New York.</p>

<p>We couldn't make it to last night's fundraiser, but we certainly want to catch up with <a href="http://www.billionairesforbush.com/about.html">this</a> fabulous group.  Politics, maybe it's not just for those other guys any more!</p>

<p>Karl Rove attended a moneyed Bush election event at a nightclub on 24th Street last night, and it wasn't always easy telling the hellfunders from the hellraisers.  The <u>NYTimes</u> <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2004/02/19/politics/campaign/19ROVE.html">reports</a> that even some of the demonstrators were confused for a while.</p>

<blockquote>
<p>"Fabulous, fabulous," Mr. Rove said as he left after giving a 20-minute talk to several hundred people gathered inside  [and collecting about $400,000 for the Bush-Cheney campaign].</p>

<p>But while Mr. Rove was inside, more than 100 protesters were outside, standing behind blue police barricades chanting slogans, waving placards and offering a bit of street theater that confused the police.</p>

<p>At one point, as hundreds of guests with invitations waited to pass through velvet barriers to enter the club, a small group of men in bowler hats and women in gowns marched up, chanting, "Four more wars" and "Re-elect Rove."</p>

<p>As the group approached, a man who appeared to be a security agent of some type, was overheard whispering into a microphone: "We've got two groups. One for and one against."</p>

<p>Actually, it was two against. The person was confused by a group that calls itself Billionaires for Bush, a collection of activists who use satire to make a political point. Indeed, members of the Sierra Club, who were protesting on the other side of the street were also confused and began shouting at what they thought was a pro-Bush contingent.</p>

<p>" We want the truth and we want it now!" the Sierra protesters shouted.</p>

<p>The billionaires shouted back, "Buy your own president!"</p>

<p>It took a few minutes, but the police finally realized what was going on when they escorted the group behind the blue barricades as well. Still, the show was not over. A black town car pulled up and out stepped a man whom who the crowd assumed to be Mr. Rove. "There is Karl Rove," people shouted.</p>

<p>Reporters, photographers and television cameramen swarmed the man, but the police pushed them back. Another man lifted the velvet rope to let him enter. But the would-be Mr. Rove walked over to the crowd of protesters and began shaking hands, when finally, again, this was seen to be a joke. It was not Mr. Rove, but an actor playing the part.</p>

<p>Each of the groups has said it planned to stage similar events when the Republican National Convention comes to New York City from Aug. 30 through Sept. 2.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>By the way, what's <a href="http://www.bettybowers.com/isbushgay.html">this thing</a> with Bush, and now Rove, regularly waving the adjective "fabulous" around like only the good fairies have been doing for years.  It's raising some fabulous eyebrows.</p>
