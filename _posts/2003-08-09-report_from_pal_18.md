<blockquote>
<p>Jayyous August 5, 2003</p>

<p>I was sad to leave Qalqilya yesterday.  The checkpoint<br />
was the usual humiliating experience.  The border<br />
guard didn't know that I could understand the abusive<br />
things he was shouting at me in Hebrew and Arabic as<br />
he demanded to inspect my backpack.  Sometimes<br />
internationals are spared the abuse.  Palestinians<br />
never are.  Then I had to quickly jump into an illegal<br />
taxi to take me to my new home.  The Israeli<br />
government policy seems to be to criminalize any kind<br />
of Palestinian movement, knowing full well that<br />
Palestinians will still move, but painting a veneer of<br />
"rule of law" onto military harassment of Palestinian<br />
civilians.</p>

<p>The next time I go to Qalqilya, we'll have to activate<br />
the Underground Railroad that our local coordinators<br />
and local volunteers have developed.  Israelis<br />
(whether Jewish or Palestinian) and internationals<br />
just don't get through the checkpoint.</p>

<p>As always, I had to change taxis at the roadblock at<br />
the village of Azzun.  A week earlier, on the way back<br />
from the Jayyous action to bring supplies to the<br />
trapped Bedouin family, we had watched soldiers and a<br />
military bulldozer (Caterpillar, natch) pile more dirt<br />
and boulders and concrete blocks onto the roadblock. <br />
It's hard to describe how degrading the scene was.  An<br />
elderly taxi driver told me that tomorrow, vehicles<br />
would be making their way around the roadblock<br />
again.he said it was like Tom and Jerry.  A soldier<br />
told me to tell my friend (Ryan, who was passing<br />
behind the bulldozer to photograph it) to be careful,<br />
remember what happened to "that girl".  He was<br />
referring to Rachel Corrie.  My blood ran cold.  I<br />
looked at him and said, "That wasn't an accident."  He<br />
shrugged.</p>

<p>Jayyous is lovely, despite the ugly scar that runs<br />
across its lands where the Apartheid Wall has been<br />
built.  Lately, Border Guards have been coming in at<br />
night and shooting water tanks on people's roofs.  My<br />
landlord here, a local activist I'll call Saleh,<br />
overheard them saying to each other, "shoot the white<br />
ones [the hot water tanks], they're more expensive to<br />
fix."</p>

<p>Jayyous, population 6,000, is not connected to the<br />
Israeli power grid.  The illegal Israeli settlements<br />
that surround it of course are connected to the grid. <br />
Power here comes from a generator, is astoundingly<br />
expensive, and is switched off every evening from<br />
5:00-7:30 and every morning from 2:00-8:00.  Cell<br />
phone coverage is poor, it's hard to keep phones<br />
charged with daily power outages, and the Internet<br />
café is slow as molasses (when the power is on-no<br />
Internet of course when the power is off).  It's hard<br />
to stay connected here.</p>

<p>We watched the sunset over the olive groves.  Jayyous<br />
is on top of a hill, with a good view of its lands, <br />
and of the densely populated coastal plain of Israel<br />
beyond.  Saleh, a mild-mannered and always polite<br />
English teacher, told us that this was the spot from<br />
which he used to throw rocks during the first<br />
Intifada, because it was hard for the Israeli soldiers<br />
to shoot kids from their position on the road below.</p>

<p>We had dinner at the home of Abu Ali, leading Jayyous<br />
activist.   He is a farmer, and served us the most<br />
amazing figs I've ever tasted.  The best ones, he<br />
explained, are the ones the birds have pecked at.  I<br />
ate one.  It was so sweet, it made my teeth hurt. <br />
(For the record, Jayyous mangoes are pretty tasty too.<br />
 I'm looking forward to the prickly pears and guava,<br />
also in season.)  Abu Ali just built a house for<br />
himself and his wife; up to now, he spent all his<br />
money sending his children to university, medical<br />
school, and the like.</p>

<p>Abu Ali camped out in front of Abu Mazen's office to<br />
get him to start talking about the Apartheid Wall. <br />
Today, he's meeting with President Arafat to demand a<br />
number of services for Jayyous's beleaguered farmers. <br />
All of Jayyous's spring-fed lands are on the "Israeli"<br />
side of the wall (any one think that's an<br />
coincidence?), and he wants the Palestinian Authority<br />
to build pipes to bring that water to Jayyous's<br />
rain-fed lands inside the Wall.  Of course, the PA<br />
can't go near a project like that without Israeli<br />
cooperation.</p>

<p>Early this morning, Israeli-American New York<br />
Palestine activist Gabriel and I took an early taxi to<br />
the village of Mas'ha, 5 kilometers east of the Green<br />
Line (West Bank-Israel border).  Mas'ha has been the<br />
site of a peace camp for several months, a place where<br />
Israelis, Palestinians, and internationals came<br />
together in dialogue and shared rage about the<br />
Apartheid Wall and the ever-expanding settlements it<br />
"protects".  Over the past days, it became clear that<br />
the Apartheid Wall was going to run east of the<br />
westernmost house in Mas'ha, trapping the family in<br />
that house between the Apartheid Wall to the east and<br />
the fence around Elkana settlement to the east.  This<br />
plan symbolizes to me the sheer brutality of the Wall<br />
project; it exists only to serve the interests of<br />
Jewish Israelis, and Palestinian people are just so<br />
many obstacles in the way.</p>

<p>Palestinian, Israeli and international non-violent<br />
activists had gathered to prevent the continued<br />
construction of the Wall at that point, and we had<br />
word that the army was coming at 7 this morning to<br />
clear them out.  Gabriel and I were late because the<br />
taxi we had booked stood us up, and by the time we<br />
arrived 47 activists were in the custody of the Border<br />
Police.  They arrested everyone with a camera first,<br />
followed by all the people who were sitting in the<br />
path of the bulldozers, followed by the people who<br />
showed up during the arrests and began filming.  They<br />
kicked people, and they dragged  them by the hair.</p>

<p>Most of the Israeli, and surprisingly, Palestinian<br />
activists have been released, but about 40<br />
internationals remain in custody at Ariel police<br />
station (Ariel is an illegal Israeli settlement of<br />
25,000 people, many of them Russian immigrants). <br />
ISM's Freedom Summer will be decimated if they're all<br />
deported.</p>

<p>I stayed in Mas'ha about half the day, watching the<br />
Israeli government's destruction.  Check out the<br />
attached photo: the houses in the background are in<br />
the illegal Israeli settlement of Elkana.  The house<br />
in the middle ground is the house to be isolated by<br />
the Apartheid Wall.  And the bulldozer in the<br />
foreground has just demolished the family's hen house<br />
to make room for the Wall.</p>

<p>I chatted with Mohammad, the owner of the house in<br />
question.  He is absolutely committed to staying in<br />
his house, no matter how impossible the Israeli<br />
government makes it for him.  We waited hours for a<br />
chance to get into the house to retrieve the backpacks<br />
of the arrestees; the construction company's private<br />
security tried to prevent us, shoving around some<br />
fierce Israeli anarchists who showed up to help. We<br />
got all the bags out eventually, and returned to<br />
Jayyous.  The future of Mohammad's family, or of the<br />
Mas'ha peace camp, is unsure.</p>

<p>Several of the Palestinian men I spoke with in Mas'ha<br />
indicated the construction of the Wall going on and<br />
said that Israel doesn't want peace.  I hear that<br />
often from Palestinians, at checkpoints, during<br />
incursions, at scenes of destruction: Israel doesn't<br />
want peace.</p>

<p>Jayyous, Friday, August 08, 2003</p>

<p>In the wee hours of Wednesday morning, the activists<br />
arrested at Mas'ha were released.  The internationals<br />
had the condition attached to their release that thet<br />
not return to the West Bank; as far as I know, they're<br />
still in Jerusalem.  Of the three who were charged<br />
with assault, the Israeli Jew was release after being<br />
charged, the Italian was released on the condition<br />
that she leave the country immediately, and Maher, the<br />
Palestinian from Mas'ha, was kept in jail with a<br />
hearing scheduled for 8 days later.  3 people, all<br />
arrested in the same place at the same time doing the<br />
same thing, all facing the same trumped up charge,<br />
with 3 different sets of rules applied to them based<br />
on who they are.  This is Israeli apartheid. <br />
Fortunately, intense pressure from ISM and from our<br />
lawyers resulted in Maher's release yesterday<br />
afternoon.</p>

<p>I did Gate Watch on Wednesday morning with John, a<br />
75-year old Englishman from Ecumenical Accompaniment. <br />
We waited at the gate that allows Jayyous farmers to<br />
pass through the Apartheid Wall to their lands.  The<br />
gate was the site of harassment and beatings by the<br />
construction company's security guards prior to the<br />
institution of Gate Watch, but is quiet now.  Only a<br />
few farmers passed through the gate, since it was<br />
blocked by boulders placed there by the construction<br />
company more than a week earlier, making passage with<br />
anything more complex than a donkey cart impossible.</p>

<p>Afterward, Gabriel and I rushed back to Mas'ha, on<br />
word that Israeli activists were about to stage a<br />
surprise action there.  About 24 young Israeli Jews<br />
arrived, held onto the scoop of a front loader, and<br />
stood on a giant drill, forcing work to stop.  They<br />
then occupied the front loader.  Their signs were all<br />
in Hebrew, and said things like "Separation Wall=Land<br />
Theft=Death", "This isn't a fence, it's a ghetto",<br />
and, chillingly, "Does this remind you of anything?" <br />
They chanted "No to the fence, no to transfer."</p>

<p>A couple of settlers watched the scene from their roof<br />
in Elkana, right next door.  I wanted to yell at them,<br />
"Aren't you ashamed?  Look at what is being done so<br />
you can have what you have!"</p>

<p>I'm told that many of the people living in West Bank<br />
settlements now are Russian immigrants.  They arrive,<br />
the Ministry of Absorption tells them, "You will live<br />
in Ariel (Elkana, Shaarei Tikva, etc.), and voila!<br />
Instant settlers.</p>

<p>Had they been Palestinians, they would have been met<br />
with live fire immediately.  Had they been<br />
internationals, they would have been quickly and<br />
brutally arrested (Freedom Summer's campaign<br />
coordinator).  But as Israelis, they were permitted to<br />
stay for a few hours, were then presented with an<br />
official order of a Closed Military Area (how a<br />
residential area can be a Closed Military Area I don't<br />
understand), and then given 30 minutes to leave. <br />
Finally, soldiers removed them, not gently, but<br />
without apparent brutality, and they were bussed to<br />
Ariel for arrest.  Ragheb was arrested along with<br />
them; he was inside the house that is going to be<br />
isolated, photographing the scene for AP.  He was<br />
released that evening, and given a paper allowing his<br />
return to Qalqilya (the checkpoint is closed at<br />
night), but his photos were confiscated.  The<br />
government of Israel really doesn't want light shone<br />
on what it's doing.  The police on the scene tried to<br />
arrest us for taking pictures, but we scrambled to the<br />
Mas'ha side of the roadblock, which they were<br />
unwilling to cross (jurisdictional issues, I think).</p>

<p>While the Israeli demonstration was going on, some of<br />
the men from Mas'ha, including the owner of the house,<br />
got into a heated argument with the head of the<br />
security staff on the scene and some of his men.  The<br />
security guy told the owner of the home, that he, the<br />
Palestinian, belonged to the past, and that all he<br />
cared about was money.  Meanwhile, the owner of the<br />
surveying firm was on the scene, fuming that work had<br />
stopped, and dynamite went off behind us as the<br />
construction company continued to clear land further<br />
south.</p>

<p>Gabriel and I proceeded to Tel Aviv, where Ady and<br />
Nirit had organized a commemoration of An-Nakba, the<br />
Palestinian Catastrophe of 1948, for the eve of the<br />
Jewish Fast Day of Tisha B'Av.  We took a bus that<br />
wound through several illegal Israeli settlements,<br />
each of them accessible through one gated entrance<br />
with an armed guard, each of them transformed from the<br />
arid landscape around them to lush irrigated suburbs,<br />
each of them far more luxurious than most Jewish<br />
residential areas inside the Green Line.  One of them<br />
was Shaarei Tikva, the settlement I had seen from the<br />
outside suffocating the villages of Beit Amin and<br />
Azzun Atme.  From the inside, the development had been<br />
designed in such a way that the adjacent villages were<br />
invisible.</p>

<p>In Tel Aviv, the commemoration went well, with a<br />
number of Israeli activists participating and thanking<br />
JAtO/NYC for taking the initiative.  We lit yahrzeit<br />
candles and arranged them to spell the number of<br />
villages destroyed in 1948, and then we read the names<br />
of all the villages.  I stayed in Tel Aviv, because<br />
one can't return to Jayyous at night.</p>

<p>John reported from Gate Watch this morning that a path<br />
had been opened up in the boulders at the gate (the<br />
way had still been blocked when we took an American<br />
visitor there yesterday afternoon), but that the gate<br />
was closed for the first time.  He was able to open<br />
it; we'll see what develops in the days ahead.</p>

<p>Jayyous, Saturday, August 9, 2003</p>

<p>Yesterday afternoon Abu Ali picked up Saleh (his<br />
nephew, turns out), me, John, Gabriel and David for a<br />
night on his farm.  We were able to pass through the<br />
gate on his tractor thanks to the small gap in the<br />
boulders in the road.  We noted however with alarm<br />
that the gate now has a chain and a lock on it, ready<br />
to be shut tight at the whim of the Border Guard or<br />
the private security.  My guess-the authorities will<br />
wait until the international attention to the Wall<br />
dissipates, and local Palestinian activists are<br />
pacified by the fear that escalation will limit<br />
access, and then they will start locking the gate from<br />
time to time, perhaps more and more often, perhaps<br />
demanding permission from farmers to be on their own<br />
land as they already have started doing in nearby<br />
Qalqilya.</p>

<p>Abu Ali's farm is right next to a giant ugly quarry<br />
gouged out of land confiscated for the settlement of<br />
Tsufim on the hilltop above.  The crater is surrounded<br />
by signs in Hebrew that say "Danger, building here." <br />
I changed one to "Destroying here."  Abu Ali showed us<br />
the blast holes that have been filled in by court<br />
order when he sued about the damage they were doing to<br />
his water tank right next door.  He told us about his<br />
8-year successful legal fight against the confiscation<br />
of his farm.  He told us about the soldier who put a<br />
gun to his head to get him to stop planting during<br />
that court fight, in front of his 8-year-old daughter,<br />
and the counseling and medication she needed as a<br />
result of the trauma.</p>

<p>The night on the farm was lovely-our own 5000 star<br />
hotel-and Abu Ali went out and picked up dinner and<br />
breakfast (supplemented by his wife's homemade<br />
goat-milk yogurt cheese).  In the morning, we visited<br />
a couple of other farms, and heard stories about<br />
recent beatings of farmers at the gate at the<br />
neighboring village of Falamiya.  Farmers with cars or<br />
trucks have been taking a big detour to use the<br />
Falamiya Gate since they still can't pas through the<br />
Jayyous Gate (the space in the boulders is only big<br />
enough for a tractor).  Tomorrow morning and<br />
afternoon, we'll expand Gate Watch to Falamiya Gate,<br />
and see what happens.</p>

<p>We passed by one of the water pumps, whose operator<br />
was targeted by the Israeli army and jailed without<br />
charges for a 4 1/2 year term as part of the Israeli<br />
government's economic war on Palestine.  David showed<br />
me where they installed a meter to make sure that on<br />
only a certain amount of water is pumped each month,<br />
while on the Jayyous lands across the Green Line that<br />
were confiscated in 1948, cotton, among the thirstiest<br />
of crops, is grown by the land's Jewish owners using<br />
unlimited water from the same aquifer.</p>

<p>Farmers are urging us to open the path to Jayyous<br />
Gate, and John, 75-year-old British Quaker, is itching<br />
to take a sledge hammer to those boulders.  But Abu<br />
Ali is urging us to wait, while he demands that the<br />
Border Guard remove the boulders as promised.</p>

<p>Abu Ali told me this morning that all international<br />
solidarity activists add to the struggle, but that<br />
Israelis are the most valuable, and foreign Jews the<br />
second most valuable.  He'll be pleased to learn that<br />
a second Israeli is joining us tomorrow from the ISM<br />
training.</p>

<p>We had lunch back at Abu Ali's farm, and were joined<br />
by 3 French and Swiss solidarity activists, as well as<br />
a Swedish diplomat and some Palestinian activists. <br />
Needless to say, lunch conversation was stimulating.</p>

<p>Back up in the village, David's been telling me about<br />
last month, when Border Guard were shooting at<br />
children, threatening Saleh's life, and shooting up<br />
water tanks as noted above.  Things are quiet here<br />
this week, but now that Israel has broken the cease<br />
fire...</p>

<p>Take a look at this media alert about a vigil tonight.<br />
 The vigil was called by one or more of the Israeli<br />
peace groups, I believe, and the eyewitness account<br />
is, I think, by an ISMer in Askar Camp, where I was<br />
last year.</p>

<p>Peace and rage,<br />
Steve</p>
</blockquote>

<p>The media alert and the eyewitness account:</p>

<blockquote>
<p><b>Media Alert - Join Us</b></p>

<p>What: A vigil to protest the recent invasion to Nablus<br />
When: Tonight, Saturday, 18:00 o'clock<br />
Where:  In front of Ministry of Defense (Hakirya),</p>

<p>Kaplan Street, <br />
Tel Aviv</p>

<p>Early morning yesterday, the Israeli Military invaded the Askar <br />
Refugee Camp in Nablus in a so-called "arrest" operation. During the invasion, four Palestinians were killed and two more are critically <br />
wounded. "We didn't mean to shoot him, we just wanted to arrest him."</p>

<p>Yes, we clearly understand that it takes seven tanks, three jeeps, a helicopter and dozens of soldiers to arrest one man.</p>

<p>The attack is yet another of Sharon's attempts to destroy the hudna and goad the Palestinians into another battle.</p>

<p>According to "Haaretz" even senior military officers admit that Hamas is not, right now, busy with preparing suicide attacks, but is complying with ceasefire plans.</p>

<p>The Sharon government is not interested in de-escalation or any sort of cease fire. He is interested in agitating for violence, so he can use it as a much-needed excuse to continue Israeli's illegal occupation and complete construction of the Apartheid Wall</p>

<p>Here's a description of what happened by an eyewitness who lives in Askar.</p>

<p><i>At 2:30 in the morning I heard a lot of people moving in the streets of the camp. Then I discovered that these people are soldiers or special forces after I heard some Hebrew words. 20 minutes later strong gunfire started, suddenly a lot of tanks, jeeps and one helicopter started to arrive to the area followed by a bulldozer.</p>

<p>The gunfire continued. I started to hear the bombs from time to time, during all this time I didn't leave my bed. After 4:30, the jeeps started to impose the curfew. After that, I received a call from my father asking me to leave my place and to join the family.</p>

<p>His fears were: in case the army started to search from house to house, having me by myself in the house would give them the chance to do what ever they want to me, as we do have long list of people who been beaten by the occupation forces taking the chances that no witnesses around. I joined my family went up to the roof to have a clear vision about what's going on, the gunfire, bombs continued, then I heard strong bombing followed with a lot of smoke.</p>

<p>After calling the neighbors to figure out what's happening, I had been told that the house of the Dwaikat family was shelled by tanks, and they destroyed the fourth and the third floor. The jeeps kept driving the  streets imposing the curfew. By six in the morning, the people started to break the curfew going out to the streets. Then confrontations started between the kids, youth, men, women, and the Army, and the army opened fire using live ammunition for the purpose of killing us.</p>

<p>Around 10:00 am I heard huge bombing.  Then it was clear that they bombed the entire building. The confrontations kept going on, and the bulldozer started to work to be sure that no one was still alive after bombing the building.</p>

<p>Around 11:30 am, the army left the camp. It was very clear there was no need for releasing the curfew as everybody was outside. I went out to see the area where the operation happened. We been told that the army took the body of the martyr Khamis abu Salim, 22 years old, whilst the people were trying to get some stuff out from under the destroyed house, they found the body of the martyr Fayez Al Sadar 28 years old.</p>

<p>All the people carried him on their shoulders toward the ambulance, everybody went home preparing himself for next day demonstration for the two martyrs. 3 hours later, 2 of the 9 who were injured in the confrontations died; Fawzi Al Alami 45 years old, and Mohammad al Tek 17 years old, and by this new news everybody started to re calculate tomorrows demonstration with four bodies instead of two!</i></p>

<p>Join us tonight.  We have to stop the occupation and the murder.</p>
</blockquote>
