<p>I met our friend <a href="http://www.johnblee.com/">John Blee</a> soon after I had moved to New York in 1985.  I knew him first as a brilliant mind and an engaging conversationalist.   I quickly found out that he was a painter and was relieved, and delighted, to learn that I really liked his work.   It might not be too much of a stretch to attribute much of my ensuing engagement with the New York art world to our friendship in the 80s.  John moved to D.C. years ago, much to my sorrow, but we never lost touch.</p>

<p>At the time we first knew each other he shared an incredibly vast unfinished loft with several truly tonic friends in what was just beginning to be referred to as "<a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DUMBO,_Brooklyn">DUMBO</a>"   It faced Manhattan, and its windows overlooking what I used to call the "electricity factory," where a transformer would occasionally act up, lighting the night sky.  Of course it made me think of the paintings.</p>

<p>John is still in Washington, but he's having a show this weekend in DUMBO, in the gorgeous apartment (with incredible views) of <a href="http://www.normajeanmarkus.com/">Norma Jean Markus</a>.  It's a return of sorts to the neighborhood which gave birth to so many beautiful canvases, even if the neighborhood itself has changed dramatically.  For one thing, a visitor from Manhattan will no longer be the only one on the street after the end of the day shift.</p>

<p>Below is an essay by writer and art critic David Matlock on John's work.</p>

<blockquote>
<p><strong>John Blee in DUMBO</strong></p>

<p>As the 100th anniversary of Kandinsky' s breakthrough approaches, it is fair to ask: what<br />
has been achieved? Are abstract paintings today repeating what has already been said--<br />
and with each repetition, fading in strength? Or do they have something new to say, both<br />
from a technical standpoint and in terms of meaning?</p>

<p>At the beginning, abstraction exploded. Kandinsky himself tried to consolidate a more<br />
controlled language and connoisseurs still argue about his degree of success. When the<br />
Abstract Expressionists adopted the language on a larger scale, canvases exploded again<br />
in shamanic frenzy. Success was hit or miss, all too dependent on possession.</p>

<p>John Blee' s first mature paintings, dating from the early 1970s, were also shamanic,<br />
painted on the floor, and dependent on force and a possessed dancing. In a career of 40<br />
years, the man has achieved total control over paint and, more importantly, now owns<br />
his meditative inscape. He owns the land that earlier painters had to burst into by force.<br />
His paintings are deliberate acts of self-intoxication. (It is worth noting, that although he<br />
came of age in the 1960s, he has always disdained the use of recreational drugs.) The<br />
Hindu and Buddhist art he experienced as a child and adolescent in India were formative;<br />
as was the medieval sculpture in the caves of Ajanta and Ellora; and the work of Indian<br />
modernists in the National Museum. Blee responds to Asian art as an insider--someone<br />
who was shaped by the culture before he received his American inheritance.</p>

<p>The paintings on display are easy to enjoy but difficult to understand. From a technical<br />
standpoint, the rendering of space is unique. There is nothing arbitrary or " atmospheric"<br />
about the backgrounds--they are architectonic--that is, they create a definite space in<br />
which " painterly event" unfolds. It is easy to take pleasure in the paint--casual admirers<br />
often remark, " What a painterly painter! What a colorist!" without suspecting the hidden<br />
narrative. I strongly suspect the hieratic " Sphinx" (2009) is one of Blee' s dogs, posing<br />
nobly on the grass--the humorous title a reference to the difficulty of knowing what the<br />
animal is really thinking. These paintings are truthful because they begin from within<br />
and encompass the outside world in an ecstatic veil of paint. Earlier abstract painters<br />
discovered a new continent; John Blee is traveling inland and is providing a faithful<br />
record of what he finds.</p>

<p>John Blee studied with Helen Frankenthaler, Robert Moskowitz, and Robert Motherwell.<br />
His paintings have been shown in Paris, Moscow, Boston, Washington DC, and New<br />
York City (including the Andre Emmerich Gallery). His work is in the Museum of<br />
Modern Art and the Los Angeles County Museum.<br />
He currently living in Washington DC.</p>

<p>- David Matlock</p>
</blockquote>
