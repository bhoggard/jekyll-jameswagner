<p>Steve Quester and his remaining four co-defendents in the trial of the <a href="/2004/10/m26_justice_has.html">M26</a> arrestees were sentenced in New York Criminal Court this morning.   It's been more than two years since their involvement in a non-violent demonstration where they joined with others in blocking Fifth Avenue, protesting the Israeli occupation of Palestinian territories and the invasion of Iraq.</p>

<p>The four are free today.  Well, sort of free.</p>

<p>They were arrested on March 26, 2003 and later that year all 16 were convicted in Manhattan Criminal Court of disorderly conduct and obstructing governmental administration [interfering with vehicle traffic].  Twelve of the activists were sentenced to community  service, a common punishment for a demonstration of this sort, and two of those twelve were given fines in addition to the sentence of community service.  The Manhattan District Attorney unsealed prior dismissed and sealed arrest records as a basis for subjecting the four remaining protestors to up to one year in jail, but on July 6 the New York Court of Appeals ordered the protestors' prior dismissed cases to be resealed.</p>

<p>Steve Quester reports that the four were sentenced this morning to 10 days each of community service.  He himself was also sentenced to pay a $500 fine in addition to his service.  There was no jail sentence, despite the District Attorney's best efforts.</p>

<p>Each of the four was also sentenced to a year of conditional discharge.  If any of them is arrested before August 1, 2006, she or he will go to jail on the M26 conviction.  As Steve writes, this is still "a scary prospect, considering that in New York City one can get hauled in by the police for walking down the sidewalk or riding one's bicycle."</p>

<p>I've copied Steve's entire pre-sentencing statement below, because it answers the question posed in this post's headline better than anything I could come up with - and because both his activism in New York and the directness of his statement are very much a piece with his volunteer work in the Middle East and his <a href="/2003/08/report_from_pal_16.html">reports</a> from Israel and Palestine published on this blog in the past.</p>

<blockquote>
<p>The Manhattan District Attorney has turned from the<br />
illegal—unsealing dismissed cases—to the merely<br />
unethical—quoting an oral history project—in his<br />
ongoing attempt to have us jailed.  As a Rutgers<br />
sociologist wrote this past Friday when she heard of<br />
the D.A.'s new sentencing letter: "Any social<br />
scientist who has done oral history would be<br />
astonished by this invasive abuse of oral history by<br />
the courts. When we do research on human subjects, we<br />
are generally obliged, by the rules of U.S. Office of<br />
Human Research Protection, to specify that none of our<br />
respondents would be harmed by the research."</p>

<p>So what's going on here?  Assistant District<br />
Attorneys Glasser and Sullivan would have us believe<br />
that Kate Barnhart and I are harmful to society, and<br />
that only a jail sentence will get the message across<br />
that we have to respect the law.  But on March 22,<br />
2004, Messrs. Glasser and Sullivan asked Judge Stolz<br />
to jail all sixteen of us, even though most had no<br />
record of any kind.  This after a trial that never<br />
should have happened; all sixteen of us offered for<br />
months before the trial to plead to a lesser charge,<br />
an offer the D.A. rejected in every one of the sixteen<br />
cases.  From what we have heard from other criminal<br />
defense attorneys, it is nearly unprecedented that the<br />
Manhattan D.A.'s office would take a case to trial<br />
that concerned allegations of non-violent civil<br />
disobedience, with no allegations of property damage. <br />
So why this time?</p>

<p>The D.A. has always maintained that we were treated<br />
differently because we were locked down.  But<br />
demonstrators using lock boxes in New York City are so<br />
common that a police officer testified at our trial<br />
about the training he received at the academy on how<br />
to remove them.  No locked-down demonstrators before<br />
or since our action have been refused a plea agreement<br />
and dragged through a trial, much less been subjected<br />
to an illegal and unethical campaign to have them<br />
jailed.  What's different this time?</p>

<p>When Mr. Morgenthau was inundated with letters and<br />
calls from elected officials, angry at the March 22<br />
2004 jail recommendation, his staff responded with the<br />
infamous April 9 2004 sentencing letter, in which jail<br />
was recommended for every defendant for whom they<br />
could dredge up any excuse, however weak.  One<br />
defendant should be jailed, they told the judge,<br />
because back in college in New Jersey he had been<br />
busted for pot.  They neglected to mention that his<br />
conviction had since been expunged.  Another should be<br />
jailed because she was once arrested in Washington,<br />
D.C.  They neglected to mention that the arrest had<br />
been nullified, without prosecution, when it came to<br />
light that the police had simply swept up a crowd in a<br />
park, regardless of individual actions.  To the credit<br />
of this court, the D.A.'s ridiculous justifications<br />
were ignored when sentencing those particular<br />
defendants in May of 2004.</p>

<p>On July 6 of this year, the system worked.  New<br />
York's Court of Appeals resealed the records of the<br />
remaining four of us, clearly establishing Mr.<br />
Morgenthau's illegal conduct.  Undaunted, Messrs.<br />
Glasser and Sullivan waited until the last business<br />
day before our sentencing to present material from the<br />
Internet they'd been sitting on since sometime before<br />
March, oral histories that establish that Ms. Barnhart<br />
and I are longtime activists.  As if that were a<br />
crime.</p>

<p>So what's behind this extraordinary sequence of<br />
events?  It's not the lockboxes.  It's not the traffic<br />
jam.  It's not our activist histories.  The same<br />
factors have been present in dozens, if not hundreds,<br />
of similar cases in Manhattan, but have never elicited<br />
similar behavior on the part of the D.A., before or<br />
since.  The unique factor in this case is the<br />
political message of our demonstration.  Ten days<br />
after the murder of American Rachel Corrie by an<br />
Israeli soldier in the occupied Gaza Strip, images of<br />
our action were beamed all over the world by CNN. <br />
Business at the branch of Bank Leumi at the corner<br />
where the demonstration occurred was disrupted that<br />
day.  Opponents of the war on Iraq often engage in<br />
civil disobedience in New York City; Palestine<br />
activists rarely do.  The events of March 26 2003 gave<br />
Mr. Morgenthau an opportunity to send a message:<br />
Criticize Israel in New York City, and we will throw<br />
the book at you.</p>

<p>Your honor, I'm not asking you to ignore the verdict,<br />
or your own concerns about the events of March 26<br />
2003.  I am asking you to consider our motives, which<br />
were to draw attention to a human rights disaster, in<br />
the context of our lives of service for the public<br />
good.  I heard, loud and clear, what you said on May<br />
12 2004 about ambulances.  I risked my life riding in<br />
a Palestinian Red Crescent Society ambulance in<br />
Bethlehem in 2002 so that the EMTs would not get shot<br />
by Israeli soldiers.  I've been a healthcare activist<br />
for a decade and a half.  I've advocated for better<br />
Medicaid coverage, for the inclusion of women and<br />
people of color in clinical trials, for expedited<br />
review of promising AIDS treatments.  I've sat at the<br />
bedside of many dying friends.</p>

<p>I have a keen sense of the need to support New York<br />
City's emergency services.  In the months following<br />
the September 11 attacks, I did what I could to<br />
support our devastated fire department.  When<br />
firefighters said that they were having trouble<br />
getting enough people to the hundreds of firefighter<br />
funerals, I began attending and bringing friends to<br />
the funerals not just of the firefighter I knew, Peter<br />
Vega, but of firefighters I'd never met.</p>

<p>The idea that we didn't consider the effect of our<br />
actions on emergency services is mistaken.  Had an<br />
emergency vehicle needed to get through that day, we<br />
would have seen to it that they did.  Nothing in our<br />
past, or in the many letters about us you've received,<br />
would indicate otherwise.  In fact, we posted<br />
observers on the scene specifically to alert us of any<br />
emergencies.</p>

<p>I am asking you as well to consider the district<br />
attorney's motives, which seem to be based in a desire<br />
to suppress advocacy on behalf on Palestinians, rather<br />
than to see justice done.</p>
</blockquote>
