<p>We were privileged to be part of the audience last night for the premier of <a href="/2003/04/a_robert_ashley.html">Robert Ashley</a>'s latest opera, "<a href="http://www.robertashley.org/productions/concrete-lamama.htm">Concrete</a>" at La Mama.  It's sad to imagine, as I do, that only in future generations will large numbers of people be familiar with the work of this giant in our midst today.    Even though it could hardly be described as "difficult" and even though its creator has been at it for almost a half century, this music is almost unknown to the people who are both the artist's muse and the only subject of his loving creation.</p>

<p>Not every one music listener today would be a candidate under any circumstances for the ranks of adoring fans of Wagner's Ring or Stockhausen's Licht cycle [I confess I am almost fanatical about both], but it may only be the tight corporate control of access to radio and television waves that can be blamed for the general public's total ignorance of the wonderful stories and music of the man who <em>invented</em> televison opera decades ago - and specifically as a popular and indigenous American art form.</p>

<p>Titles like these (from his catalog) are not made for an elite, and should not be kept in its possession:  "Your Money My Life Goodbye", "Purposeful Lady Slow Afternoon", "Interiors Without Flash", "Outcome Inevitable", "Improvement (Don Leaves Linda)" and "Music Word Fire And I Would Do It Again (Coo Coo): The Lessons".  The music is even lovelier.</p>

<p>Performances of "Concrete" continue through Sunday.</p>

<p>Last night at the end of his rich daydream odyssey one of the voices of this semi-autobiographical opera's protagonist sighs,  "I tell stories and sing;  I've nothing else to do."</p>

<p>NOTE:  "concrete" is here an abstraction for the poet's cherished city</p>
