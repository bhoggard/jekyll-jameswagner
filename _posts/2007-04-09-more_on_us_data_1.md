<p>It's not the first story like this we've heard, it's not the most disturbing, and unfortunately it won't be the last, but it's worth a read.  It's an account of one young man's personal and repeated experience of our nation's obsession with terror and it comes from a Canadian artist whose work Barry and I both know appreciate and who is with a New York gallery whose programs and directors we both respect and love.</p>

<p>The story was sent to me in response to the first of my <a href="/2007/04/well_need_your.html">two recent posts</a> about the eye and face-scanning databasing program run by our troops in Iraq.</p>

<p>[The writer had written that he did not want it published, but as it turns out not for any reason I would have imagined.  I asked him if I might have his permission to publish it anonymously.   He answered yes, but added that the reason he would like his name withheld was not the fear of political consequences, but rather, "i don't really think that artists should enter the dialogue with people who write about art."  I am printing his words here in their entirety and without editing the text I received, except to eliminate  information which might identify the author.]</p>

<blockquote>
<p>i live in [Canada], and came to NY for the armory a few months ago.  i had<br />
brought some watercolours with me that i hadn't finished in time to ship to<br />
the gallery.  i had done this MANY times before when coming to the US. as<br />
you can imagine from my name, i'm just a WASP male, but i have a rather<br />
mujahadeenish beard, grown not for fashion, but years ago, to hide a total<br />
lack of chin. anyway. i had some watercolours tucked into a magazine in my<br />
carry on. i declared them on the customs form. after going through the US<br />
customs on the canadian side, i was asked to go into a secondary screening<br />
room. this has happened to me many times before also, i am always getting<br />
'randomly' searched, which i think might have to do with coming from<br />
[a town with a reputation for drug use]. however this time, i<br />
was told that bringing paintings into the country was illegal, i explained<br />
that i had done it many times before with no problem, they told me that was<br />
the fault of the customs agents who were derelict in their duty by allowing<br />
such a transgression of the law . i explained that selling my work is my<br />
only income, and that i wouldn't do it again etc etc. long story short, i<br />
have gone my entire life without being fingerprinted, i have never broken<br />
the law, gotten a ticket. however after i was told that i would be able to<br />
bring my work to NY, this time, but NEVER again, and if i tried it again, i<br />
would be in real trouble, i was subject to retinal eye scans, i had all of<br />
my thumb and fingerprints scanned, and a biometric photograph of my face<br />
taken. so now i am also one of the canadians in the homeland security file.<br />
unlike people like mahar arar who was sent to syria for torture, or omar<br />
khadar who is languishing in cuba, arrested as a 15 year old for throwing a<br />
grenade at a soldier in iraq, i hopefully won't be extraodinarily<br />
renditioned, unless they discover a secret scottish-irish mujahadeen. but,<br />
i have a solo show in NY in september, i am less and less inclined to visit<br />
the US. this is my fifth trip in a row where i was randomly singled out.<br />
the first time i was subject to documentation. most of my friends in canada<br />
are of pakistani or indian descent. i grew up with them in toronto. canada<br />
is a multicultural place, save quebec, not a melting pot. and it really<br />
opened my eyes to what my friends must have to deal with. so i thought this<br />
story might be interesting to you. i'm starting to think i might pull a<br />
marlon brando and start sending the other forgotten victims of american<br />
imperialism to my openings for me, the sacheen littlefeathers of my first<br />
nations neighbours . . . .</p>
</blockquote>
