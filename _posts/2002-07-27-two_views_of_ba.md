<p>The Barcelona AIDS Conference can be seen as the latest, problematic worldwide <a href="http://www.actupny.org/reports/bcn/">attempt</a> to come to terms with what is expected to be the worst epidemic in human history*.  Unfortnately it can also be seen through the demented eyes of <br />
those who have condemned themselves to the sickness of ignorance and hate.</p>

<p>Most people, even in the United States, are blissfully unaware of the extent of the pure hatred and viciousness which has always been thrown at queers and, in the last twenty years, at those who have been perceived as representing HIV disease (for these fools' purposes, the two groups are indistinguishable), but even after the death of tens of millions, this particular horror, produced by the hatred which arises from our lowest instincts, remains.</p>

<p>Distinct from the vituperation hurled at what is thought of as "The Other" is that cast by some gays and HIV positive people themselves at those whom they consider impolite or impolitic troublemakers.</p>

<p>Upon returning from Barcelona this month, one member of ACT UP found in the group's mailbox an avalanche of emails revealing a perverse literature produced by blind hatred.  I have seen some of the messages themselves** and they are incredibly sick, and the "stack" includes messages from gays as well.</p>

<p>Here is another ACT UP correspondent's take on the phenomenon, written in response to an email distribution of the message texts:</p>

<blockquote>
<p>So very, very sad. These poor ignoramuses have no idea what AIDS [activism] has done for patient empowerment, for public-private cooperative community-based interventions, for basic virology, for holding drug companies accountable and causing them to change their course, for getting research spread across several institutes of NIH integrated....</p>

<p>Of course, the unifying thread in all these letters is homophobia. I<br />
don't think I have heard as many variations of stick it up your ass<br />
since I went to a conference on anal-genital neoplasms. It's<br />
interesting. These horrid, hateful people were always here, but they<br />
seem to have dropped the "...and the poor babies" bit.</p>

<p>Based on what I infer of these authors' intelligence and compassion<br />
after reading these letters, I would say pissing  them off was a sign<br />
you're doing the right thing.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>* <b>an item from the <u>Chicago</u> <u>Tribune</u></p>

<p></b>FACING UP TO AIDS WORLDWIDE</p>

<p>July 26, 2002</p>

<p>Viewed as a whole, the global AIDS epidemic is a calamity so immense it<br />
 likely will leave you feeling powerless. You're tempted to turn to other,<br />
 more manageable problems. It defies the imagination.</p>

<p>As of the end of last year, approximately 40 million people worldwide were<br />
 living with HIV, the virus that causes AIDS, and 22 million more had   <br />
 already<br />
 died of the disease. At the present rate of growth, the number of infected<br />
 people is likely to rise to 100 million by decade's end, according to the<br />
 latest batch of grim statistics released by the United Nations earlierthis<br />
 month. Add the collateral damage--children left without parents, economies<br />
 without workers, countries without futures--and the AIDS epidemic takes on<br />
 apocalyptic proportions.</p>

<p>But surrendering to AIDS by not mounting an effective global response<br />
 amounts to virtual suicide. The answer lies in breaking up the challenge<br />
 into more comprehensible pieces and acting accordingly, as participants at<br />
 the recent International AIDS Conference in Barcelona attempted to do.  <br />
 Some<br />
 essential elements:</p>

<p>- A strategy has to be both global and coherent. This is a challenge<br />
 custom-made for the UN if there ever was one, and Secretary General Kofi<br />
 Annan's initiative to develop a global fund to coordinate money-raising,<br />
 determine priorities and collect reliable information about the scope of <br />
 the disease is the right place to start.</p>

<p>- Money--in the tens of billions of dollars--will be needed. Annan has  <br />
 set a<br />
 yearly target of $7 billion to $10 billion, also targeting malaria and <br />
 other<br />
 killers. Depending on who's doing the arithmetic, the U.S. has contributed<br />
 as much as $1 billion or as little as $200 million.</p>

<p>The real number is probably somewhere in between--and not enough. Annan<br />
 would like the U.S. to contribute $2.5 billion yearly. The size of the<br />
 American economy--and the economic and security implications of AIDS in<br />
 Africa and of the exploding number of cases in Russia, China and India-- <br />
 make<br />
 Annan's number a good down payment on what the U.S. ought to contribute.</p>

<p>- The debate over prevention versus treatment in developing countries is<br />
 past. Both are needed: Treatment of those already infected is an integral<br />
 part of the process of preventing additional infections.</p>

<p>- Beating up on the big bad pharmaceutical companies is self-defeating- <br />
 they<br />
 are the ones coming up with new treatments. Instead, international<br />
 dual-pricing agreements ought to be established to maintain the financial<br />
 incentive for further research while making drugs affordable to poorer<br />
 nations.</p>

<p>- At this juncture, proven and blunt prevention methods--distribution of<br />
 clean hypodermic needles, condoms and sex education, among others--have <br />
 to<br />
 take precedence over moralizing. In Russia, the number of cases is nearly<br />
 doubling every year. Most of the victims are drug addicts who use dirty<br />
 needles. In the U.S., clean-needle distribution has repeatedly been   <br />
 stymied<br />
 by some conservative groups despite its effectiveness. That is not an   <br />
 option<br />
 worldwide.</p>

<p>- Local leadership is essential. While some underdeveloped nations have <br />
 been<br />
 ravaged by AIDS, Brazil has set an example of how head-on strategies of<br />
 treatment, prevention, education and other interventions can begin to    <br />
 lower<br />
 the rate of infection. Global efforts cannot bear fruit amid local apathy.</p>

<p>The UN fund to fight AIDS deserves both active American participation and<br />
 dollars. Doing otherwise flies on the face of fundamental human compassion<br />
 and our clear national interest.</p>

<p>Copyright (c) 2002, Chicago Tribune</p>
