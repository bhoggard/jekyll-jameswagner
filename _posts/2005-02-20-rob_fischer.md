<p>Cohan and Leslie has another winner with Rob Fischer's <a href="http://www.cohanandleslie.com/content.php?scr_w=896">current show</a> [site not updated] of sculptures, painted photographs and paintings.  Once engaged, it's hard to walk away from this space, the images and the entire environment are that compelling.</p>

<p>The very healthy-looking grasses shown in both images included here are a part of a stunning piece which, in the words of the press release, "makes 'the outside' suddenly containable."  It includes</p>

<blockquote>
<p>. . . a tire track cut across Fischer's yard accidently which has been excavated whole and installed in a metal tray.</p>

<p>. . .</p>

<p>Fisher's project [that is, the entire show] addresses and explores the tension between transience and memory and the specifics of site.</p>

<p>. . .</p>

<p>The photographs on view, painted C-prints shot by the artist from his car while driving through his native Minnesota, are images of abandoned trailers on fire from various viewpoints.  This creates a cinematic yet disorienting effect when viewed from one to the other.   The trailer, an American icon of a culture that is historically characterized by the desire to migrate and discover is seen in an indefinable state - partly present, partly destroyed.</p>
</blockquote>

<p><br /><br />
<img alt="fischerrobtrack2.jpg" src="/mt_archives/fischerrobtrack2.jpg" width="450" height="600" /><br />
Rob Fischer <em>Summery (Goodyear Ecology)</em> 2004-05 tire track excavated from cultivated swamp, mixed media 32" x 71" x 30"</p>

<p>As I child I delighted in constructing new or re-imagining found miniature environments, so <em>Summery</em> totally charmed me on more than one level.  I wanted to bring this one home.  Barry suggested we could commission <a href="/2004/07/studio_360.html">Eric Doeringer</a> to do a "bootleg" work in a more convenient, apartment size.  Hmmmm.</p>

<p>I kept expecting to see a tiny frog or guppy show up in the track, and the sight and gentle sound of water trickling from the pipe, if normally unnecessary to represent an ordinary puddle, was a delightful recognition of the requirements of the unnatural venue.</p>

<p>There is still much more, including a small warren of rooms constructed so that their interiors are less than two feet wide, each one separate from the other yet connected by a maze of water pipes.  These spaces suggest domesticity, but with a built-in architecture of unease.  New Yorkers will get it right away.</p>
