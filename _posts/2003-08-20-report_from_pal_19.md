<p><b>Gates As a Form of Collective Punishment</b></p>

<p>Jayyous, Wednesday, August 20, 2003</p>

<p>Ras Atiyah's gate has been closed since the<br />
demonstration last Friday.  The people of Ras e-Tira,<br />
Dab'a and the other communities walled in with Alfe<br />
Menashe have been cut off from Palestinian life,<br />
apparently as punishment for their daring to hold a<br />
peaceful demonstration in favor of children's access<br />
to school.</p>

<p>Today, every gate in the Apartheid Wall in the West<br />
Bank, from Tubas to Jenin to Tulkarm to Qalqilya to<br />
Mas'ha, is chained shut.  Thousands of Palestinian<br />
farmers are unable to reach their land.  The reason? <br />
A member of Islamic Jihad from Hebron in the southern<br />
West Bank, in retaliation for the Israeli army's<br />
assassination of an Islamic Jihad activist in Hebron<br />
last week, carried out a dastardly attack on<br />
ultra-Orthodox Jewish worshippers in Jerusalem<br />
yesterday.  The connection with Palestinian farmers in<br />
Jayyous?  Absolutely none.</p>

<p>None of the 35 farmers who lined up at the gate  in<br />
Jayyous this morning had anything to do with events in<br />
relatively far-off Hebron and Jerusalem.  Most of them<br />
probably condemn the attack in Jerusalem, as well as<br />
Sharon's provocations.  But all of them were kept off<br />
of their land, as were farmers up and down the<br />
northern West Bank.</p>

<p>The idea here is of course not to make Israelis safer.<br />
Preventing a farmer in Jayyous from planting his<br />
seedlings carries no benefit for Jews in Jerusalem<br />
wishing to return home safely from the Western Wall. <br />
What the closure does is make life in Palestine that<br />
much more unlivable, Palestinians' access to their<br />
land and water that much more tenuous, "voluntary"<br />
ethnic cleansing that much closer to reality.<br />
That's the real purpose of locking the gates in the<br />
Wall.</p>

<p>I'm sorry I haven't been in touch for a while.  Here's<br />
my journal for the last ten days.  No photos - the<br />
computers in the Internet cafe in Jayyous won't let me<br />
attach.</p>

<p>--Steve</p>

<p>Jayyous, Sunday, August 10, 2003</p>

<p>"Nabil", ISM coordinator from Tulkarm, is in Jayyous<br />
for a visit.  He told us a hair-raising story last<br />
night.  He was taken from his house and imprisoned at<br />
age 17Å“  during the waning days of the first Intifada.</p>

<p>He said that the Israeli forces used to target the<br />
top students for arrest.  He was only in jail for 21<br />
days, a very short term compared to  most of the<br />
Palestinian men I know, but during that time the<br />
guards went from underground cell to underground cell,<br />
opened a hatch in the very heavy metal door, and<br />
dropped in a half kilo of powder with an action like<br />
tear gas, only stronger.  (I assume that the Jews<br />
reading this journal have the same horrible<br />
association that I have with this image, even though<br />
the substance in question here is not Zyklon B and is<br />
usually not lethal.)   Nabil says that it took about<br />
80 hours for the irritant to dissipate.  "Rashid",<br />
head of the Prisoners' Club in Qalqilya, had told us<br />
about the same process in all 3 of the Israeli prisons<br />
he was in.  Last week, about 100 prisoners at Ashqelon<br />
Prison were injured while being gassed in their cells,<br />
9 of them critically.</p>

<p>Gabriel and I went to the Falamiya Gate this morning,<br />
and then walked over to the Jayyous Gate with John. <br />
Very few farmers passed through either gate.I wonder<br />
if a lot of them are sleeping in their fields beyond<br />
the wall these days?  The Border Guard was patrolling<br />
and there were security at the Jayyous gate where<br />
workers with heavy equipment (Caterpillar, natch) were<br />
digging a trench, but we didn't see any harassment. <br />
It's a tough call deciding whether or not to do Gate<br />
Watch.one doesn't want to get up at 5:30 in the<br />
morning to waste one's time, but we'd be devastated if<br />
there were another incident at a time that we could<br />
have been there.</p>

<p>Walking back up to the village, a man stopped us and<br />
told me about his 70 dunums of land taken from him by<br />
the Wall.  He referenced boulders on his land and the<br />
nearby settlement of Tsufim, so his land must be near<br />
the quarry.  He said, "Can you help me?"  I can't, of<br />
course, and we both walked away feeling pretty bad.<br />
A new crop of ISM activists came into Jayyous<br />
today-all Jews, including Liat, a staff member of<br />
Jewish Voices for Peace in San Francisco, Ora from<br />
JAtO/NYC, and Rann, an Israeli who lives in Britain.</p>

<p>Qalqilya, Monday, August 11, 2003</p>

<p>Today, ISM activists from all over Palestine once<br />
again converged on Qalqilya for a big demo against the<br />
Wall.  The demo was called by the Qaliqilya political<br />
parties in response to Democratic members of the U.S.<br />
House of Representatives who visited Israel as guests<br />
of the American Israel Political Action Committee.and<br />
who didn't visit the wall.  It was the usual <br />
Underground Railroad scenario, and all of us, each in<br />
a small group, managed to slip in undetected.  We<br />
spent a lot of time making signs and props for the<br />
demo.  (There are two ways to get media: someone gets<br />
shot, or activists get creative.  We prefer the<br />
latter.)  One activist drew a picture of Qalqilya<br />
being hanged by a noose (representing the Apartheid<br />
Wall that surrounds it), with an Israeli soldier<br />
holding the noose.  The soldier was drawn as an evil<br />
looking guy with a big nose and Stars of David on<br />
him.it looked more like a Nazi propaganda poster than<br />
a sign for Palestinian freedom.  David from JAtO/NYC<br />
pointed it out to me, I said something, and the sign<br />
was trashed.</p>

<p>Tracie called me from Jayyous to say that some farmers<br />
came to the house to look for us-there's a bulldozer<br />
in the orchards beyond the fence knocking down lemon<br />
trees.  One of the farmers stood in front of the<br />
bulldozer and stopped the work for the afternoon.<br />
Tracie, Michelle and David went out to the orchards,<br />
where an elderly farmer was holding branched of a<br />
ruined lemon tree and wailing "Allahu akbar" over and<br />
over again.</p>

<p>Jayyous, Tuesday, August 12, 2003</p>

<p>Today was the 2nd big Qalqilya demonstration at the<br />
wall.  In the morning, we heard on the news about the<br />
attacks in Rosh Ha'ayin and Ariel (undoubtedly a<br />
response to the much deadlier Israeli army attack on<br />
Askar Refugee Camp on Saturday night), and prepared<br />
ourselves for soldiers with a hair trigger. The<br />
Israeli authorities in the meantime closed Qalqilya<br />
checkpoint, meaning that no media could get in to<br />
cover our action.</p>

<p>We had many more participants from Qalqilya than last<br />
time.  We marched to the vicinity of the military gate<br />
in the southwest corner of what's left of Qalqilya,<br />
just like last time.  This time, instead of hoisting a<br />
giant banner with balloons, we used paint rollers on<br />
very long poles to paint giant Palestinian flags up<br />
above the graffiti from July 31.  We also tied strings<br />
of names of prisoners to tennis balls, and threw them<br />
over the wall, and girls from Qalqilya released doves.</p>

<p>Check out <a href="http://www.palsolidarity.org/">www.palsolidarity.org</a> for photos.</p>

<p>Our two negotiators, Lisa from Italy and Sam from the<br />
U.S., were awesome.  At one point, a few boys from<br />
Qalqilya threw stones in the direction of the troops<br />
who were watching the demonstration with guns cocked. <br />
Despite the fact that older boys and men from Qalqilya<br />
exposed themselves to the troops by running out front<br />
and literally tackling the stone throwers in order to<br />
stop them, the Israeli soldiers began throwing sound<br />
grenades at us (not exactly a de-escalatory tactic). <br />
The usual Israeli army tactic is to follow the sound<br />
grenades with tear gas, then rubber bullets, and<br />
eventually live ammunition.  Sam and Lisa, however,<br />
who had positioned themselves among the soldiers from<br />
the beginning of the demo, convinced them to stop<br />
throwing the grenades and allow the demo to wind down<br />
naturally.  There were a few speeches and we withdrew.</p>

<p>As we left, Israeli soldiers were spotted in hiding<br />
in the orchards off to the side (something the<br />
commander denied to our negotiators), which was a<br />
provocation that sparked more stone throwing and tire<br />
burning.  Fortunately, the army, withdrew, and no<br />
Palestinian boys were hurt.</p>

<p>Tel Aviv, Wednesday, August 13, 2003</p>

<p>In the morning, Rann and Ora and I from ISM went down<br />
to the gate in the Apartheid Wall near the village of<br />
Falamiya-the next gate to the north after Jayyous's<br />
gate.  The gate was open (although, ominously, the<br />
same chain and lock that had recently appeared on<br />
Jayyous's gate was hangin on this one), farmer traffic<br />
was very light, and there was no military  presence in<br />
evidence.  We chatted with a farmer working on is<br />
trees right inside the Wall, who spoke with us in<br />
Hebrew.  He said that there would be a process<br />
regarding the Wall and its gates: first the gates are<br />
always open.  Then the gates are always open, but<br />
there's a lock and chain hanging on it.  Then they're<br />
locked at night.  Then they're locked during the day<br />
as well, except for certain hours.  Finally, the gates<br />
are locked for good, paving the way for the<br />
confiscation of all the land outside the fence.  We've<br />
heard these same predictions from a number of farmers;<br />
when you've lived under occupation for 36 years, you<br />
tend to know the ways of the occupier.</p>

<p>Later in the afternoon, the internationals from ISM<br />
and Ahmed and Farouq from Jayyous Peace Activists went<br />
down to the orchards where lemon trees were being<br />
bulldozed.  It seems that a farm road is being paved<br />
and widened-for reasons we can't understand and quite<br />
against the wishes of the farmers whose trees are<br />
being destroyed.  The farmers we met, however, asked<br />
the Jayyous Peace Activists and ISM not to mount a<br />
demonstration.  They noted that the workers widening<br />
the road are Palestinian, and were being careful not<br />
to damage irrigation pipes or trees other than the<br />
hundreds in the path of the widened road.   They felt<br />
that any protest against this unnanounced invasion of<br />
their land would cause the contractors to replace the<br />
Palestinian workers with Druze or Jewish workers who<br />
would carelessly destroy anything in their path.  They<br />
were also afraid that any protest would lead to a<br />
retaliatory locking of the gate; now that the<br />
Apartheid Wall is up, the Israeli army has enormous<br />
control over Palestinians' behavior just by holding<br />
the keys to the gate.</p>

<p>A group of us New Yorkers spent the evening in Tel<br />
Aviv (the privilege to pass freely between Israel and<br />
the West Bank of course being one that our Palestinian<br />
friends and hosts don't have).  We went to the Tel<br />
Aviv Beer Festival because D.A.M., a Palestinian<br />
hip-hop artist from Haifa whom we admire for his<br />
political songs, was scheduled to appear on the<br />
Israeli hip-hop and reggae stage.  We spent a few<br />
hours there, but were so disgusted by the acts that<br />
preceded D.A.M. that we had to leave.  Some were just<br />
amateurish and stupid, but others were pro-occupation<br />
hip-hop artists who rapped about crushing Gaza among<br />
other things, and who were introduced as having "blue<br />
and white flowing in their veins".  Ugh.</p>

<p>Jerusalem, Thursday, August 14, 20003</p>

<p>I went up to Jerusalem early in the morning for an ISM<br />
Core Group meeting in El-Ram, a Palestinian West Bank<br />
community illegally annexed to Israeli Jerusalem.  It<br />
was wonderful to see Lysander from New York's Direct<br />
Action Palestine there; I hadn't seen her since she<br />
was arrested in Mas'ha and banned from the West Bank. <br />
The meeting was long and involved; ISM is an ambitious<br />
international project committed to grassroots<br />
democracy and consensus process.  I had to miss the<br />
second day of the meeting because of my region's<br />
scheduled action in Ras Atiyah.</p>

<p>Jayyous, Friday, August 15, 2003</p>

<p>Early in the morning I got on a bus chartered by Yesh<br />
Gvul, a 20-year-old Israeli organization for military<br />
refusers, to go to a demonstration in Ras Atiyah<br />
organized by the Committee Against the Wall/Committee<br />
Against the Settlements with ISM participation.  It<br />
was interesting to talk with Itai, an organizer from<br />
Yesh Gvul, during the ride.  He downplayed the time he<br />
and others have spent in jail for refusing their<br />
Israeli military service, noting that it's the<br />
Palestinians who live in a jail all the time.</p>

<p>I also talked with other ISM internationals on the bus<br />
about the previous night's house demolition in Askar<br />
Refugee Camp in Nablus, where I spent time last year. <br />
The house demolished was under ISM's protection, and<br />
the internationals in the house chose to stay with the<br />
family when the family decided to evacuate (as per the<br />
orders of the Israeli soldiers who arrived in the<br />
middle of the night).  It was hard news to take for<br />
the many Nablus ISM members who would have wanted to<br />
be there to help, but were in Jerusalem either to<br />
attend the Core Group meeting, or because of their<br />
arrest in Mas'ha.</p>

<p>As I've written here before, Qalqilya is entirely<br />
surrounded by the Apartheid Wall.  South of Qalqilya<br />
is a corridor outside the wall that allows passage<br />
from Israel to the illegal settlement of Alfe Menashe,<br />
east of Qalqilya.  South of that are the villages of<br />
Habla and Ras Atiyah, themselves surrounded by the<br />
Wall.  The Wall has been constructed in such a way<br />
that 3 Palestinian villages and 2 Bedouin settlements<br />
have been walled in with Alfe Menashe. 1000 people in<br />
these 5 communities have been cut off from access to<br />
the rest of the West Bank, but forbidden to enter<br />
Israel.  The stage is set for ethnic cleansing there.</p>

<p>Ras e-Tira is one of those 5 communities in the Alfe<br />
Menashe enclave created by the Wall.  Children from<br />
Ras e-Tira go to school in Ras Atiyah, which is in the<br />
Habla-Ras Atiyah enclave created by the wall.  The<br />
school in Ras Atiyah is right next to the Wall<br />
(children painted slogans against the Wall's<br />
construction on the school), and the kids from Ras<br />
e-Tira have to pass through a gate there in order to<br />
reach the school.  They are often prevented from doing<br />
so, and the demonstration was planned to call<br />
attention to Ras e-Tira's lack of access to education.</p>

<p>We were met at the demonstration site by the usual<br />
private security paid to guard the construction of the<br />
wall. They were their usual aggressive selves, but we<br />
were able to put a protective line of internationals<br />
and Israelis in front of the Palestinian demonstrators<br />
who held signs, chanted, sang, and made speeches<br />
against the Wall.  The security called the army and<br />
the Border Guard, who showed up and demanded that<br />
everyone withdraw inside the gate.  We were asked by<br />
the organizers not to give up the gate, because it<br />
would be closed and locked, stranding the<br />
demonstrators from Ras e-Tira.  It was pretty tense<br />
for a while, as soldiers and Border Guard gave us five<br />
minutes to disperse before they began using force, but<br />
our negotiators were wonderful, and Palestinian and<br />
international demonstrators showed incredible<br />
discipline in the face of verbal and physical<br />
provocations by the soldiers, Border Guard, and<br />
security.  The Israeli demonstrators were not as<br />
disciplined, and one got into  a verbal confrontation<br />
with a security guard that ended with the guard<br />
slapping the activist, after which the activist<br />
(natch) was arrested.  They also tried to arrest<br />
Jackie, a videographer from New York, but we swallowed<br />
her up into the crowd (but not before a soldier<br />
grabbed and broker her camera).</p>

<p>As the time for Friday prayers approached, the<br />
Palestinian activists prayed in the schoolyard rather<br />
than going to the mosque, which put the soldiers and<br />
Border Guard in a bind.  When they were done, Ras<br />
e-Tira demonstrators were permitted to cross the gate<br />
and return to their village.  Jackie crossed too,<br />
since her bags were in Ras e-Tira, and the Border<br />
Guard tried again to arrest her.  Fortunately, she got<br />
away.  We then withdrew into the schoolyard, and the<br />
Israeli soldiers closed and locked the gate.<br />
The Jayyous Peace Activists were really happy with the<br />
demonstration, and we're looking forward to more work<br />
with the villages south of Qalqilya.  One suggestion<br />
from a member of the Land Defense Committee in Ras<br />
e-Tira is that we set up a fence in the schoolyard in<br />
Ras Atiyah to help ensure kids' access once school<br />
starts on September 1.</p>

<p>At 7 in the evening, a boy came to our door to tell us<br />
that the gate in the Wall had been closed and locked,<br />
and many farmers were stranded outside.  We walked<br />
down the hill, and found Abu Ali there among the<br />
others, waiting to get out to his lands to spend the<br />
night.  He called the mayor, who called the Israeli<br />
army District Commanding Officer, and at about 8:30<br />
soldiers came by and opened the gate for the people<br />
waiting to get in and out.  I took a flask photograph<br />
while they were re-locking the gate behind the last of<br />
the farmers, which enraged the commander of the<br />
soldiers.  He demanded repeatedly to know who I was,<br />
but I was long gone.</p>

<p>The soldiers said that they'd be opening the gate for<br />
a few hours each morning and evening from now on. <br />
It's hard to describe how humiliating the whole<br />
experience was, with farmers on their own land locked<br />
out of their own homes by a capricious and vindictive<br />
foreign army, an army that's been here for 36 years.</p>

<p>Jayyous, Saturday, August 16, 2003</p>

<p>I descended to the gate four times today, as it was<br />
locked, unlocked, locked and unlocked again, each time<br />
with a different story of what hours it would be open.</p>

<p>Tracie and Michelle, ISM Jayyous, told me about our<br />
friend "Mortaza" and why he stays away from the gate<br />
and from demonstrations-he was arrested, held tied to<br />
a chair for 72 days, and then imprisoned for 17<br />
months.  He was never charged.  He's been told that if<br />
he's seen "causing trouble", he'll be reimprisoned.</p>

<p>On Gate watch I chatted with "Abd el-Wahid", who told<br />
me about his grandfather's land, now in Tel Aviv, and<br />
how he still has the Ottoman deed to it.  Tracie and I<br />
also had tea with Khaled, a shepherd who was taken by<br />
the army a month ago while tending his flock outside<br />
the fence, and taken to Jaljulya checkpoint and<br />
released at 3:00 in the morning.</p>

<p>Liat (of the Bay Area's Jewish Voices for Peace) and I<br />
went to visit the Bedouin family trapped outside the<br />
Wall.  We were pretty worried about walking through<br />
Jayyous's south gate, since Palestinians and<br />
internationals have been threatened and shot at for<br />
passing through, but we thought it was important to<br />
check on the welfare of this family.  The mom and some<br />
of the kids were home, and they enthusiastically<br />
welcomed us and served us tea as we sat and talked<br />
amongst their rabbits and chickens (dad was out with<br />
the sheep).  They're running low on water, because the<br />
pump in the nearby village of Azzun is broken.  That<br />
pump directs water from the aquifer to villages all<br />
over the area.  People in the village buy water ($150<br />
for 11 cubic meters), but they can't get the tank<br />
delivered from the other side of the Wall (not to<br />
mention the obstacle of the money).   The mom has<br />
diabetes, and had medical papers indicating her<br />
permission to pass through to Qalqilya for treatment,<br />
but the security guards ripped up her papers and<br />
denied her passage.  She was also worried about the<br />
kids' ability to pass through the gate to get to<br />
school next month.</p>

<p>We committed to having internationals walk the kids to<br />
and from school each day starting in September, on the<br />
assumption that the security, soldiers and Border<br />
Guard would hesitate to harass them with<br />
internationals present.  We also went back to the<br />
Jayyous peace activists, who said they'd talk to the<br />
mayor about trying to get permission from the District<br />
Commanding Officer to have a tractor with the water<br />
tank cross the gate.  We committed to raising the<br />
money.</p>

<p>When we returned to the gate, we saw that boys who had<br />
seen us walk through the gate ventured to do the same<br />
themselves, and set tires on fire in the road.  We<br />
were pretty mad about it, because of the danger it put<br />
us in, but the boys were nowhere to be found.  We<br />
passed through the gate unmolested.</p>

<p>The farmers were locked out of Jayyous again tonight,<br />
and the mayor had to call the DCO to send soldiers to<br />
open briefly.</p>

<p>Qalqilya Sunday August 17, 2003</p>

<p>During Gate Watch this morning, some Palestinian<br />
construction workers who are building the Apartheid<br />
Wall sat down with us to chat (needless to say,<br />
they're not from Jayyous).  I kept my distance from<br />
them.  Israel's economic war on Palestine is so severe<br />
that some Palestinians will build their own cages for<br />
a daily wage.  Later in the day, a reporter and<br />
photographer from a regional newspaper in the Sharon<br />
region of Israel came to interview people, see the<br />
Wall, and met the Bedouin family.  When the two<br />
journalists, accompanied by a group of Jayyous Peace<br />
Activists and internationals, tried to return to the<br />
village, we found the gate closed and blocked by one<br />
of the security guards.  After we stood in the sun<br />
arguing with them for a while, a jeep came along and a<br />
soldier ordered the security guard to open the gate. <br />
He told the two Israeli journalists (he didn't know<br />
that one of the internationals is also Israeli) that<br />
they couldn't go back into Jayyous without permission.</p>

<p>This is nonsense, of course; Jayyous is in Area C<br />
according to the Oslo Accords, which means that there<br />
are no restrictions on Israeli access.  Nevertheless,<br />
the journalists chose not to go back to Jayyous with<br />
us.and accepted a ride with the soldiers in the jeep. <br />
Now I understand why Palestinians distrust Israeli<br />
journalists.  What would we think if a Daily News<br />
reporter interviewed people in Red Hook about police<br />
brutality, and then got a ride out of there in a<br />
police car?</p>

<p>In the evening we were called to Qalqilya to help with<br />
a situation there.  A giant military bulldozer had<br />
shown up with a surveyor, attempting to destroy all<br />
the greenhouses, sheds, and farmlands within 50 meters<br />
of the 8-meter high wall on the west side of Qalqilya.</p>

<p>The bulldozer stopped working and left when<br />
internationals arrived on the scene, but was expected<br />
to return during the night or the next day.  ISM<br />
activists converged on the city to block the<br />
destruction.  We decided that we would not allow tear<br />
gas, rubber bullets, or threat of arrest keep us from<br />
blocking the bulldozer.</p>

<p>Jayyous Monday, August 18, 2003</p>

<p>We waited at a greenhouse by the Wall in Qalqilya,<br />
watching a military bulldozer work outside of the<br />
Wall.  It never came in to destroy the lands within 50<br />
