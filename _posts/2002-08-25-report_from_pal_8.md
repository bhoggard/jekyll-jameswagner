<p>[Donald just sent this message describing Steve's report of his experiences yesterday and today in Palestine.]</p>

<blockquote>
<p>I am writing this around 10:30 AM on Sunday, August 25, New York Time</p>

<p>This report covers Saturday, August 24 and some of Sunday, August 25.</p>

<p>As reported previously, a demonstration protesting the occupation was <br />
being planned for Saturday in a town called Hawara (sp?).  <br />
Internationals from ISM were joining Palestinians, along with an <br />
Israeli group from Jerusalem, Ha Taayush.</p>

<p>Approaching Hawara, the ISM contingent was met by soldiers.  These <br />
soldiers did not advise the group of anything, they simply started <br />
detonating sound bombs and launching tear gas.  Steve says he got <br />
mildly tear gassed.</p>

<p>The groups were split up as they dispersed, and Steve ran into an <br />
olive grove.  It was while he was doing this that I happened to make <br />
my usual morning call.  He told me he would call me back.  A few <br />
minutes later, I heard from him.  He was in the olive grove, and told <br />
me everything I have just written.  He said he had been invited to <br />
someone's home and was going there.  While he was in the olive grove, <br />
Palestinians came out with onions, which can provide some relief from <br />
tear gas when you sniff them.</p>

<p>While he was in Hawara at this person's home, tanks went through the <br />
streets with bullhorns and shouts of "Curfew", while launching <br />
teargas.  Steve and his hosts locked their doors and shuttered their <br />
windows and decamped to their hallway.  Some little boys were brought <br />
in off the street.  Steve helped the little boys and they helped <br />
back, offering their onions, as well as rags soaked in vinegar (which <br />
helps alleviate the stinging of tear gas).</p>

<p>Later, when Steve was in the town, he spoke with a woman, saying what <br />
a shame it was that little boys were getting tear gassed.  She <br />
said "Tear gas is nothing.  You can get over tear gas.  Bullets are <br />
the problem".  Steve realized that it was possible that the soldiers <br />
might have used live ammunition if it weren't for the presence of <br />
internationals.</p>

<p>Steve and the ISM people never met up with Ha Taayush.  Ha Taayush <br />
was physically stopped by the soldiers from entering the town.  They <br />
weren't tear gassed but as Steve put it, they were manhandled.  They <br />
weren't allowed to march to the checkpoint, but were allowed to hold <br />
a rally.  Then they went back to Jerusalem.</p>

<p>So how to get back home to Nablus and Askar refugee camp?  Steve and <br />
about 30 other internationals (plus two Palestinians) got in cabs as <br />
started a journey along a settler road.  Eventually they were stopped <br />
by the Army because the cabs had West Bank plates and weren't allowed <br />
on the road.  The soldiers made everyone get out of the cabs.  It was <br />
unclear what would happen next.  Obviously, they would have to walk <br />
to the village of Iraqborin.  But before they could do that, the <br />
soldiers wanted to check everyone's ID.  This would have guaranteed <br />
arrest of the two Palestinians.  So Steve and others fluent in Hebrew <br />
argued and obfuscated and generally prevented IDs from being checked.</p>

<p>They were all welcomed very graciously by the people of Iraqborin and <br />
spent Saturday night there.</p>

<p>One of the Palestinians in the group had not been allowed out of <br />
Nablus for more than a year.  She was very happy just to have been <br />
out for a little while.</p>

<p>On Sunday they went back into Nablus.  This wasn't supposed to <br />
happen.  Steve says the soldiers are preventing internationals from <br />
getting into Nablus and trying to remove all the internationals who <br />
are inside.  Apparently, soldiers are stopping everyone, even <br />
searching ambulances, and not allowing them through if they contain <br />
internationals.  Steve believes that the military is planning a big <br />
action in Nablus and does not wish to be fettered by humanitarian <br />
concerns which internationals might call attention to.  Nevertheless, <br />
with the help of some seasoned drivers who knew all the right dirt <br />
paths to circumvent checkpoints, Steve was delivered to the door of <br />
the Nablus office of the Union of Palestinian Medical Relief <br />
Committees.</p>

<p>Steve is going to rest today.  He was exhausted by Saturday's <br />
activities.  Tonight he will stay in Askar refugee camp, and tomorrow <br />
he will go back to Bethlehem to visit people and bring things to them.</p>

<p>Steve says "It's amazing how hard life is here under curfew.  You <br />
risk imprisonment just for going out to get food."</p>

<p>That's the report.<br />
dsg</p>
</blockquote>
