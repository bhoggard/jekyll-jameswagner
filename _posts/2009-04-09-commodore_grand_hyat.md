<p><em>ghosts in the night:  the Pan Am Building kisses the Commodore Hotel</em></p>

<p>I had originally intended to post this virtually abstract shot as an image alone, with no commentary, like I do with many of my photographs, but then I got to thinking about some of the follies that these two barely-seen structures (the Met Life Building* reflected in the windows of the Grand Hyatt Hotel) represent, and what they continue to tell us about New York's past.  Finally, during a long-anticipated visit to the new Hearst Tower last Wednesday I looked out a window in the northeast corner of one of the higher floors and I realized that some of us haven't learned a thing.  I now knew how I was going to finish this post.</p>

<p>Almost 30 years ago the Hyatt Hotel group demonstrated that there really <em>are</em> second acts in New York, but they may not always be worth staying for, or even bearable.  The early twentieth-century "skyscraper" which stood on the Hyatt site, adjacent to Grand Central, was until 1980 known as the <a href="https://www.flickr.com/photos/modashell/2986373925/">Commodore Hotel</a>.  Not surprisingly, it had been named for "Commodore" <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cornelius_Vanderbilt">Cornelius Vanderbilt</a>, the railroad entrepreneur who built the first Grand Central Terminal in 1871.</p>

<p>The Hyatt corporation's architects <a href="https://www.flickr.com/photos/10961326@N07/3101311264/">retained the shape</a> of its mass, and left most of the exterior bricks of the old hotel in place, merely covering everything with a highly-reflective glass skin.</p>

<p>Anyone who thinks this kind of philistine rape is a thing of the past might be advised to take a walk across town.   An equal or even greater abomination is being committed between Eighth Avenue and Broadway in the upper fifties.   I'm referring to what owner/developer <a href="http://www.muckety.com/Joseph-Moinian/6982.muckety">Joseph Moinian</a> calls "3 Columbus Circle".  Originally known as "Columbus Tower"**, when it was finished in 1928 (Shreve & Lamb, architects), the building occupies the entire block, between 57th and 58th Streets.   Some will remember It once sheltered the much-missed <a href="http://www.nysun.com/new-york/coliseum-books-to-leave-gaping-cultural-wound/40684/">Coliseum Books</a> inside its southeast corner.</p>

<p>Last week I saw huge sections of masonry gouged out of finely-laid brick walls every few feet of the building's surface, all destined to hold brackets for a totally-redundant glass curtain wall.  I couldn't keep looking, and, inexplicably, didn't take any pictures.  Maybe I couldn't imagine looking at them once I got home.  For those with the stomach, here's the <a href="http://www.3columbuscircle.com/">website</a> devoted to the building's transformation and marketing.</p>

<p>The site of this commercial-developmental obscenity is cater-corner from the bold, newly-topped-out Norman Foster <a href="/2005/01/hearst_tower_di.html">Hearst Tower</a>, which shoots out of the cast-stone facade of the six completed floors of the <a href="http://www.nyc-architecture.com/MID/MID124.htm">landmark</a> 1928 Joseph Urban-designed New York Hearst headquarters.  But it will bear a dramatically closer affinity to the new facade tacked onto <a href="/2003/11/save_2_coumbus.html">2 Columbus Circle</a>, one block north of it, a monstrous work of destruction commissioned by the institution which I mischievously continue to refer to by its original name, the "<a href="http://www.madmuseum.org/">American Craft Museum</a>" (and not just on account of its notorious architectural crime).</p>

<p>The <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pan_Am_Building">Pan Am Building</a> may not be anyone's favorite New York skyscraper, but at least it's still permitted to represent something other than a shiny siding job.</p>

(1963) architects: Emery Roth & Sons, with Walter Gropius and Pietro Belluschi</p>

<p>**<br />
New York Times architecture critic David Dunlap has dug back even further.  In a board post [with interesting pictures] on <a href="http://wirednewyork.com/forum/showthread.php?page=4&t=16497">Wired New York</a>, he writes:  "For now, a palisade of three-story Ionic columns, supporting a neo-Classical entablature, surrounds the base of the structure. This is a visible vestige of the Colonnade Building, designed by William Welles Bosworth . . . .</p>

<p>Shreve & Lamb’s brown-brick facade was far simpler than the monumental colonnade. That incongruous combination of ornate base and spartan tower still speaks subtly — to anyone patient enough to listen — about the rise of <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2000/07/07/arts/street-of-automotive-dreams.html?scp=1&sq=dunlap+automobile+row&st=nyt">Automobile Row</a> in the early 20th century. But in a few months, it will be gone; another quirky corner of Manhattan that has been scrubbed, smoothed, polished, branded and lost."</p>

<p><strong>NOTE:</strong>  The image is of the west wall of the Grand Hyatt, showing a few white-ish rectangular windows;  the smaller, more numerous blue-ish shapes are the lighted windows in [what I normally call] the Pan Am Building, reflected on the Hyatt glass.   The photo was taken from the sidewalk on the south side of 42nd Street.</p>
