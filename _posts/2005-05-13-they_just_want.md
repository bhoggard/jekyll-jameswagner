<p>I'd love them even if they didn't sing, but they do, and now we're learning how much they want to, and <em>why</em> they want to.</p>

<p>Because of the beautiful garden which is enclosed by our two relatively-low-rise Manhattan apartment buildings lying along the great eastern flyway, we can easily imagine that we live inside an aviary.  I recognize the sounds of sparrows, blue jays and mourning doves, and I'm proud and delighted to know the distinct call of the male cardinal who shares the courtyard with a mate who is almost as red as he is, and sometimes he comes to visit our own small garden annex one story up, but I have no idea who is producing the other delightful sounds which come from our wonderful arboretum/sanctuary.</p>

<p>The other afternoon, just after a guest had remarked upon the quiet of our apartment (at least the north side), I found myself resting on a bed near an open window trying to determine what I could detect of the machine sounds of the great city which surrounds our walls.  &nbsp;&nbsp; . . . nothing.  Instead I found myself enchanted by what seemed to be an avian composition more exotic than usual, composed mostly of percussive clicks acccompanied by a gentle whistle (think <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Martin_Denny">Martin Denny</a>'s "exotica").  When it's the real thing, and you don't need mosquito netting, it's sheer ecstasy.</p>

<p>This morning while reading the papers I came across one of those odd news/feature reports which manage to overshadow everything else I may have read that day, the sort of find which makes reading hard copy news still worth its demands in time and forearm disturbance.</p>

<p><u>New</u> <u>York</u> <u>Newsday</u>'s staff writer Jamie Taylan (undoubtedly another bird lover, but then who isn't?) <a href="http://www.nynewsday.com/news/local/manhattan/nyc-hssong134256252may13,0,2026095,print.story">reports</a> that birds raised in isolation can learn a complex tune not part of their heritage but will switch to their mating song once spring arrives, even if they've never heard it before.</p>

<blockquote>
<p>Scientists at Rockefeller University in Manhattan found that young male canaries raised in the lab had no trouble learning a computer-generated song that had no resemblance to the song their father would normally teach them.</p>

<p>But one morning, the scientists arrived at the lab to discover that the birds, on the brink of adulthood, were chirping the song they were destined to sing - even though they had never heard it before. The research appears today in the journal <u>Science</u>.</p>

<p>. . .</p>

<p>Somehow, [former post-doctoral student at Rockefeller Timothy J.] Gardner said, they do their own editing, splicing and rearranging of the computer-generated song so that they are singing the song, speaking the language understood by songbirds.</p>

<p>. . .</p>

<p>Gardner and his colleagues, including Rockefeller neuroscientist Fernando Nottebohm, say that the mature birds in the experiment sing their species-specific song, yet every once in a while the old riff from their youth can be heard.</p>

<p>. . .</p>

<p>Nottebohm said that the ability of the songbirds to sing two distinct types of song "is reminiscent of people speaking two languages and being able to use both. Not a small feat for birds."</p>
</blockquote>

<p>But, sitting next to a tiny animated parakeet by a window at the edge of a garden filled with his chattering distant relatives, at least the news about two languages doesn't surprise me at all.</p>
