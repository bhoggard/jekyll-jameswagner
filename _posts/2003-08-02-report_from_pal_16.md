<p><a href="https://jameswagner.com/index.php?m=200307#955">Steve</a> writes from the West Bank:</p>

<blockquote>
<p>Qalqilya<br />
Aug. 2, 2003</p>

<p>At 3:00 last Friday morning, I was awakened by Kevin,<br />
a member of the ISM Qalqilya action group, and a local<br />
photojournalist I'll call Ragheb, and told that the<br />
Israeli army had again entered Qalqilya.  Four of us<br />
went out to see what was happening.  After the<br />
shocking attack on a house earlier in the week, we<br />
felt that it was important that we be present, albeit<br />
at a distance, visible, and out of the line of fire,<br />
in the hope that international witnesses might inhibit<br />
the Israeli army from their worst excesses.</p>

<p>We proceeded to the main street, and spoke to a few<br />
people who were out.  An old man said to me, "Why do<br />
you want to drink from that cup?"  Others called out,<br />
"Thank you for what you're doing."   Some young men<br />
told us that the soldiers had been firing into the<br />
air.</p>

<p>A jeep and an armored personnel carrier (APC) entered<br />
the road from a side street.  Both were completely<br />
enclosed, with tiny reinforced windows, so that it was<br />
impossible to see the human beings inside.  The APC<br />
stayed at a distance, and the jeep stopped with its<br />
bright lights on us for a long time.  Then they sped<br />
away.</p>

<p>We continued down the street in an attempt to find the<br />
house(s) being raided.  The jeep and APC returned, and<br />
stopped a block away.  We're pretty sure we were<br />
visible to the jeep.  A few shots were fired.  A<br />
volley of machine gun fire followed, and in the dark<br />
we couldn't tell if the gunfire was directed at us, at<br />
the buildings opposite the vehicles, or in the air.  <br />
I said, "This is how Brian Avery got shot."  We got<br />
out of sight of the army, and returned to our house. <br />
We continued to hear sporadic gunfire as the two<br />
vehicles sped up and down the street.  On Friday<br />
evening, we all had a long talk about going out at<br />
night when the army is in town, and decided that we<br />
would only consider going out if we had specific<br />
information about where the army was and what they<br />
were doing.  Marwan, our local coordinator, said that<br />
there's nothing we can do if the army has come to<br />
arrest someone, but if they're planning to demolish a<br />
house, we might be of use.</p>

<p>On Friday afternoon we visited two of the houses that<br />
were raided.  In the first, the wanted man was not<br />
present, and we saw the usual scenes of gratuitous<br />
destruction, although the house was not riddled with<br />
bullets like the one we saw previously.  As usually<br />
happens in these situations, there were children in<br />
the family who were eager to take us from room to room<br />
to show us the damage and to bring us spent shell<br />
casings.  The family had "Peace Now" stickers in<br />
almost every room, and the soldiers had tried to rip<br />
one of the stickers off the wall.</p>

<p>The adults in the family told us that a large number<br />
of jeeps and soldiers had shown up, and that the<br />
entire family had been made to stand outside for<br />
hours.  Apparently, the one jeep and one APC we saw<br />
shooting up downtown were distractions designed to<br />
keep folks scared and in their houses.  I wonder if<br />
the soldiers were wearing white sheets.</p>

<p>At a second house that was raided we were invited to<br />
stay and drink tea and coffee.  Their son was taken,<br />
and they didn't know where.  We gave them the number<br />
for HaMoked, an Israeli human rights organization that<br />
tracks Palestinian detainees.</p>

<p>In the news in the United States, we hear about the<br />
three-month cease fire.  It seems to be that the<br />
Palestinians are the only ones holding their fire.</p>

<p>I got a couple of hours of sleep after we returned<br />
from our failed intervention, and then headed out for<br />
a day in the villages south of Qalqilya with Samir, of<br />
the State Information Service (sort of a statistics<br />
and publishing office) and Courtney, Lysander and Lisa<br />
from our action group.  Samir is from Habla, a village<br />
immediately south of Qalqilya.  His family came there<br />
from Arabia in the 17th century, and was the first<br />
family in Habla.  He can name every other family in<br />
the village, and where they came from (usually other<br />
places in Palestine).</p>

<p>We needed to walk through another farmers' gate - it's<br />
now had hurricane fencing installed, so that one can<br />
no longer crawl through it.  We waited until an APC<br />
drove away, then tried to open it, but couldn't figure<br />
out the mechanism.  We stepped back to figure out what<br />
to do, when a little boy on a bicycle flipped open the<br />
gate.  We ran across the 100 meters of empty space to<br />
the Israeli army's gate on the road into the village<br />
of Habla.  Samir was waiting for us there.</p>

<p>Samir took us around the nurseries there, and a<br />
nursery owner told us the now-familiar story of the<br />
ruin of his once thriving business.  We had tea and<br />
grapes with Musa, a cousin of Marwan, our local<br />
coordinator.  He told us how Israeli soldiers had come<br />
to him while he was working and demanded to see a<br />
permit to be working the land.even though he owns that<br />
land himself.</p>

<p>The construction site of the "security fence" wound<br />
around this area of nurseries, orchards, and farms,<br />
cutting it off from Habla and from Qalqilya.  At one<br />
point, we walked right up to the Green Line (the<br />
border between Israel and Jordan between 1949 and<br />
1967), and crossed over it.  The fence doesn't<br />
separate Israel from the West Bank at all; it annexes<br />
West Bank land to Israel. A farmer with a tomato field<br />
just over the Green Line into the West Bank showed us<br />
how his crop had been ruined when Israeli settlers<br />
released pigs into his field two weeks earlier.</p>

<p>Samir brought us to a spot where a front loader and a<br />
number of workers on foot were putting up the fence at<br />
breakneck speed.  He explained that this was his land,<br />
now all consumed by the fence.  The workers on foot<br />
were Palestinian.work is so hard to come by for<br />
Palestinians that they will take jobs building their<br />
own prison.  Samir is a gentlemanly and businesslike<br />
man, but when we were on his ruined land, he seethed. <br />
We took photos of his irrigation pipes, dug up and<br />
tossed aside by the front loader.</p>

<p>Ragheb took a photo of the Jewish Israeli operator of<br />
the front loader, and armed security showed up and got<br />
right up in his face, screaming at him in Hebrew and<br />
demanding his camera.  He didn't give it up, and they<br />
didn't lay a hand on him.perhaps because Courtney,<br />
Lysander, Lisa and I were there.</p>

<p>We went to look at a centuries-old mosque in Habla. <br />
The security fence was just feet away from the mosque,<br />
with an Israeli settlement across the road.  Samir<br />
explained that this particular portion of the fence<br />
had been built in 1996, four years before the<br />
beginning of the Al-Aqsa Intifada.</p>

<p>We spent midday at Ras Tira, a hilltop village across<br />
the ravine from the sprawling Israeli settlement of<br />
Alfe Menashe.  Opened in 1982 and expanding<br />
constantly, Alfe Menashe now has 40,000 inhabitants<br />
and sits on top of the region's main aquifers (one<br />
Jewish Israeli West Bank settler is allotted twenty<br />
times as much water as one West Bank Palestinian<br />
resident).  At the bottom of the ravine, Israeli<br />
soldiers hold military exercise, and when villagers<br />
attempt to approach their olive trees further down the<br />
slope, soldiers shoot at them.</p>

<p>Ras Tira is one of several villages, with a combined<br />
population of 1000, being fenced in with Alfe Menashe<br />
and cut off from any other West Bank communities. <br />
They have been given until 2005 to accept Israeli ID<br />
cards or leave the village.  Many of them have ID<br />
cards now that list a place other than Ras Tira as<br />
their home.  I doubt those people will be given the<br />
option of Israeli ID.</p>

<p>Ras Tira has already been erased from Israeli<br />
government databases.</p>

<p>We stopped at a giant road block next to the<br />
Israeli-only highway that cuts through Habla on the<br />
way to Alfe Menashe.  The only way to pass from one<br />
side of Habla to the other is on foot.  I stood at the<br />
side of the road for a long time, looking at the faces<br />
of the drivers passing by, trying to will them into<br />
realizing that everything they have is at the direct<br />
expense of someone else.</p>

<p>We went to Samir's home for a sumptuous lunch.  He and<br />
his wife both work in Qalqilya, and keep an apartment<br />
there because the passage to and from Habla is too<br />
difficult to do every day.  Habla is a few hundred<br />
yards from Qalqilya.</p>

<p>When we got back to the gate into Qalqilya, it was<br />
locked (no army around), and lots of people were<br />
milling around on the Habla side.  Then a boy-he must<br />
have been about 10-opened the gate, and all of us,<br />
men, women, kids on bikes, someone on a horse, rushed<br />
through before soldiers showed up.  The kid seemed<br />
delighted when each of us stopped to thank him.</p>

<p>On Saturday, we were invited to the end-of-summer-camp<br />
presentation of the Palestine Red Crescent Society. <br />
One of the skits was about an ambulance stopped at a<br />
roadblock as the patient dies. The little kids who<br />
played the mean Israeli checkpoint soldiers were<br />
intense.</p>

<p>On Saturday night, Basem, an ISM volunteer, invited<br />
the men among us to celebrate his having passed the<br />
tawjihi, the extremely rigorous end-of-twelfth-grade<br />
comprehensive set of exams.  The party was in what<br />
looked exactly like a Brooklyn wedding palace, and was<br />
absolutely packed with deliriously happy young men<br />
dancing to traditional Arab music.  At one point, they<br />
began chanting as they danced "kus uchtak yaa Sharon"<br />
(fuck you, Sharon).  They were thrilled when we joined<br />
in.  After the party, we went for a midnight swim at<br />
one of Qalqilya's 2 pools-men only, of course.</p>

<p>On Monday, while four from the Qalqilya action group<br />
(including Dena and Eric from JAtO) were participating<br />
in the "break the gate" action in Annin, outside<br />
Jenin, that got international attention, another four<br />
of us (including me and Ryan from JAtO) met up with<br />
David and Nirit from JAtO, other internationals from<br />
ISM, Boston to Palestine, and others, and activists<br />
form Jayyous in another delivery of supplies to the<br />
Bedouin family trapped outside the Jayyous fence.  The<br />
army stayed away, realizing, I think, that they had<br />
provided the media with unfavorable photo ops the week<br />
before.  The delivery went off successfully, but it<br />
was incredibly sad to see an old woman from the family<br />
standing at this enormous fence, waiting for handouts.<br />
 She allowed the media to interview her and photograph<br />
her, and while she was talking to them I saw that she<br />
was crying.  In the attached photo, she's on the<br />
right.  A man and boy from the family are loading up a<br />
donkey with the supplies thrown down to a Jayyous<br />
activist.  All three entered the no man's land between<br />
the razor wire and the fence for the purpose of the<br />
delivery.  I don't know the names of anyone in the<br />
Bedouin family.  I couldn't speak to them.  I was<br />
behind the razor wire barrier with everyone else.</p>

<p>Again, the land "inside" the fence in this picture is<br />
in the West Bank.  The land "outside" the fence is<br />
also in the West Bank.  This is not a border fence<br />
between Israel and the territory it's occupied for 36<br />
years.  Those of us opposed to the fence believe it<br />
has a two-fold purpose:  incorporate valuable land and<br />
water resources outside the fence into Israel,<br />
decreasing the resources available to (and therefore<br />
the viability of) a future Palestinian state, and to<br />
pursue a policy of ethnic cleansing of areas inside<br />
the fence, as Palestinians, deprived of income, are<br />
forced to leave.  The more Palestinians leave, and the<br />
more Israeli Jews arrive as settlements are expanded,<br />
the more occupation becomes annexation.</p>

<p>On Wednesday, international activists arrived in<br />
Qalqilya from Ramallah, Jenin, Tulkarm, Jerusalem,<br />
Nablus, Jayyous, and elsewhere to participate in the<br />
Qalqilya face of the week's Wall actions.  None were<br />
allowed through the checkpoint.  Some snuck through<br />
the checkpoint when soldiers were otherwise occupied. <br />
Some crawled under a farmers' gate in the fence.  Some<br />
snuck through another gate.  Some stayed overnight in<br />
a nearby village after failing to get in (and nearly<br />
getting arrested), and succeeded upon trying again<br />
early in the morning.  And some were unable to enter<br />
at all.  Ady (JAtO) and Tim from our action group were<br />
also able, with help from arrangements Marwan made and<br />
accompaniment from Ragheb, to get 8 big helium tanks<br />
(!) into town.</p>

<p>Thursday was the big day - the payoff after<br />
approximately one million planning meetings with<br />
everyone in Qalqilya from the mayor on down, and with<br />
each other, after creating a giant banner ("No<br />
apartheid wall" in English, Hebrew and Arabic)<br />
designed to fly 15 meters above the ground, after<br />
filling countless balloons with oil paint to fling at<br />
the wall (and then discovering that oil paint corrodes<br />
latex-oops), and after browbeating the press from Tel<br />
Aviv to Toronto (they usually wanted to know if there<br />
was going to be bloodshed).  We marched from the<br />
municipality in the center of town-50 internationals,<br />
and Qalqilyans from the Prisoners' Club, local<br />
government, the PFLP, the Peasants' Union, and many<br />
others-to the point at which the wall meets the fence,<br />
joined by a military gate and a sniper tower.  It was<br />
a beautiful site as activists flung paint balloons at<br />
the hated wall, and covered its surface (the lower<br />
half, at least) with messages of liberation.  We were<br />
met by soldiers in jeeps who had their guns at the<br />
ready, but when they saw that the line of<br />
internationals facing them was neither advancing on<br />
them nor heeding their orders to disperse, they chose<br />
restraint.  It's entirely possible that restraint was<br />
a policy insisted on from above, considering that the<br />
photo of soldiers teargassing activists in Annin on<br />
Monday went all over the world.</p>

<p>The giant banner flew only briefly before the balloons<br />
popped, but long enough for some good photos.  It will<br />
now hang from Ash-Sharqa Girls' School next to the<br />
Wall, a school that has been teargassed in the past by<br />
the Israeli army while the students were present.</p>

<p>There was a fair-sized crowd of Israeli activists from<br />
Gush Shalom and other groups outside the military<br />
gate.  They got short notice from us about the demo,<br />
but filled a bus for their companion demo nonetheless.<br />
 We ended with Noura, Palestinian-American from ISM,<br />
delivering a message of peace to them (which was<br />
permitted after a lot of wrangling with the soldiers0.</p>

<p>A lot of the Qalqilyans I've spoken with are very<br />
pleased about the demonstration, although some had<br />
hoped for a larger turnout of locals.  I think that,<br />
now that we've proven ourselves, we'll have a bigger<br />
turnout next time.</p>

<p>Yesterday (Friday) activists in Tulkarm succeeded in<br />
removing the razor wire in front of a gate there, but<br />
were unable to tear down the gate before the Israeli<br />
army opened fire with rubber bullets.  There were a<br />
number of injuries among activists, but none serious. <br />
I stayed in Qalqilya, holding down the fort as it<br />
were.  I joined the Prisoners' Club at the Qalqilya<br />
Zoo (the only zoo in the West Bank) for a party for<br />
the children of prisoners, and in the evening, after<br />
the rest of the group returned from Tulkarm, we<br />
participated in another loud, spirited, multi-party<br />
march through town in support of prisoners.  It seems<br />
that yesterday prisoners in Ashkelon Prison were tear<br />
gassed while locked in their cells, resulting in 100<br />
injuries, 9 serious.  The president of the Prisoners'<br />
Club named for me the 3 prisons in which he was tear<br />
gassed while locked in his cell.</p>

<p>Today, Saturday, we'll be talking about our work in<br />
the surrounding villages.  I may be moving to nearby<br />
Jayyous, in which case my telephone service will be<br />
spotty (but not nonexistent).  I'm not sure about<br />
email access there...I'll check it out when I arrive<br />
(assuming the move happens).</p>

<p>That's all for now.</p>

<p>--Steve Quester</p>
</blockquote>
