<p><a href="https://jameswagner.com/index.php?m=200307#955">Steve</a> reports on his last 6 days in Palestine and Israel.</p>

<p>The subjects, in order, include Ramallah, Black Laundry, checkpoints, "refuseniks," his new all-gender affinity group "faygelach," a Tel Aviv court, the farming village of Jayyous, the Apartheid Wall, aquifers, Qalqilya, the wonderful and sad Amal Society for the Deaf, "extrajudicial executions," and his friends's chagrin about being generously feted rather than actively useful in the communities so far.</p>

<blockquote>
<p>Qalqilya, occupied Palestine<br />
Wednesday, July 16, 2003</p>

<p>We went to Ramallah on Friday and Saturday for ISM<br />
training.  To get into Ramallah from Jerusalem, we had<br />
to pas through the Qalandia checkpoint.  The<br />
checkpoint is a huge affair, with giant concrete<br />
blocks arranged into mazes for incoming and outgoing<br />
people, lots of Israeli soldiers scrutinizing people<br />
passing through, and an Israeli sniper tower overhead.<br />
 Where once people could just drive between Jerusalem<br />
and Ramallah, two of the most important cities and 20<br />
minutes apart, there are now giant clogged parking<br />
fields at either end for the taxis taking people to<br />
and from the checkpoint.  We got through without any<br />
problem because they were only checking people leaving<br />
Ramallah on Friday, but the whole experience was<br />
incredibly stressful.  I can't imagine what it's like<br />
for people who live in Ramallah.</p>

<p>There were about 25 of us trainees, from the U.S.,<br />
Scotland, England, France, and Canada.  Quite a few of<br />
us were Jewish, including one Israeli-American and one<br />
Orthodox woman.</p>

<p>Our trainers were a member of Jews Against the<br />
Occupation/New York and a member of the Michigan Peace<br />
Team.  They facilitated role play and discussion about<br />
commitment to non-violence, how to de-escalate in the<br />
face of settler and soldier violence, how to protect<br />
Palestinians and ourselves non-violently, what to do<br />
if arrested, and so on.</p>

<p>George Rishmawi, a co-founder of ISM from the<br />
Palestinian Center for Rapprochement Between Peoples<br />
in Beit Sahour, Palestine, talked with us about the<br />
history of non-violent resistance in Palestine and<br />
about Palestinian cultural norms in the communities in<br />
which we will live and work.</p>

<p>A member of Black Laundry, Israel's gay, lesbian,<br />
bisexual and transgendered activists against the<br />
Occupation, talked about his military service in the<br />
Occupied Territories and Lebanon, and why he now<br />
prefers to serve time in a military prison rather than<br />
serve the reserve duty required of every Israeli<br />
Jewish man until age 55.  While in prison, he met one<br />
active duty soldier who was in prison for 6 months for<br />
smoking a joint, and another who was in for one month<br />
for killing an elderly Palestinian civilian.  The<br />
death had been deemed an accident, but the prisoner<br />
said that his commanding officer knew that he had<br />
killed the old man on purpose.  He also met fellow<br />
"refuseniks" who had served their active duty during<br />
the Intifada of the late 80s and early 90s.  He said<br />
that they were gentle, intelligent, educated men who<br />
had never talked about the things they had done in the<br />
army, not even to their wives, until their time in<br />
prison with him.  He was amazed at the terrible things<br />
they had done, and the trauma they were still living<br />
with.</p>

<p>Neta Golan, an Canadian-Israeli co-founder of ISM who<br />
now lives in Nablus with her Palestinian husband and<br />
their baby came to speak with us, baby in tow, about<br />
arrests, deportations, and legal issues.  She also<br />
spoke about the weaponry that the Israeli army uses<br />
against peaceful protest, and how to respond safely to<br />
tear gas, sound grenades, rubber bullets, moving<br />
vehicles, shooting over out heads, and live fire aimed<br />
at demonstrators.  (The way to respond safely to live<br />
fire is to remove oneself from the situation<br />
immediately.)</p>

<p>Two Palestine activists from New York helped us work<br />
on how to use the media to get the message out.</p>

<p>Ramallah is a bustling, prosperous town, now that it<br />
is not under Israeli military curfew, although one<br />
must still pass through Israeli checkpoints (more like<br />
choke points) to get there.  We had a nice time there<br />
on Friday evening, eating shawarma and ice cream, and<br />
visiting an Internet café.</p>

<p>By the time we left, our affinity group had<br />
crystallized.  An affinity group is a small group of<br />
activists who know one another well and trust one<br />
another, who work as a unit in planning and<br />
implementing direct action.  There are 6 of us in my<br />
affinity group, all from New York, all of whom have<br />
organized together before, and we named our mostly<br />
queer, mostly Jewish group Faygelach for a Free<br />
Palestine (faygelach is Yiddish for faggot, although<br />
we intend it to mean queers of all genders).  For the<br />
JAtO and DAP folks on this list: the group is me,<br />
Eric. Lisa B., Amy Laura, Ady and Lysander.  Dena will<br />
be joining us in a few days, Ryan a week after that,<br />
and Gabriel from DAP in early August.  We are hoping<br />
that Ora and Ramzi will be joining us as well.</p>

<p>Leaving Ramallah was a little tricky, because the<br />
Israeli soldiers suspected that our Israeli-American<br />
member was Israeli.  She pretended not to understand<br />
Hebrew, and was eventually waved through despite her<br />
lack of a visa.  Our taxi was then stopped at a<br />
traffic checkpoint, where the soldiers said they were<br />
going to hold us for a while so they could check out<br />
the two of us with U.K. passports.  Must have had<br />
something to do with the guy they were looking for in<br />
the West Bank, who's a peace activist, but who the<br />
authorities are claiming is an IRA bomb maker. <br />
(Thankfully, he has no connection to ISM; I'm sure<br />
they'd love to use him to smear us.)</p>

<p>On Sunday, a large number of ISM activists, consular<br />
officials, and Israeli peace activists went to court<br />
in Tel Aviv for the trial of 8 ISM activists awaiting<br />
deportation.  (The American, French, Swedish, Danish,<br />
and British consulates were all contacted because the<br />
arrestees come from all those countries.  Only the<br />
Swedes and Danes bothered to show up to support their<br />
citizens.)  The judge was clearly impressed to see a<br />
full courtroom; deportation proceedings usually<br />
involve workers from Nigeria, Thailand, and other<br />
Third World countries who have replace Palestinian<br />
menial labor in Israel since the Intifadas.  They<br />
usually sit in prison while their deportations are<br />
adjudicated in writing.</p>

<p>The case has been put off for a few days or longer,<br />
and the 8 young men continue to be held in the police<br />
station at Ariel, one of Israel's illegal West Bank<br />
settlements.  We did win a preliminary injunction<br />
preventing the deportations while the case is being<br />
decided; one of the Ministry of the Interior's<br />
favorite tricks is to put people on a plane in the<br />
dead of night while their deportation cases were being<br />
argued.  Hopefully this injunction will prevent them<br />
from doing so.  We of the Feygelach have volunteered<br />
to contact folks to write affidavits attesting to<br />
ISM's non-violent mission, and have had a good<br />
response from Israeli and international individuals<br />
and organizations, including members of the Knesset.</p>

<p>On Monday, we traveled from Tel Aviv to Jayyous, a<br />
West Bank farming village close to the pre-1967<br />
border.  We had to travel to an Israeli road block<br />
where trucks have to back up to each other on either<br />
side of the road block and their cargo has to be hand<br />
carried from one to the other.  We got into Jayyous,<br />
where we met almost all the men, women and children of<br />
the town, as well as about 40 internationals, for a<br />
march initiated by the farmers.</p>

<p>Jayyous lost 90% of its land when the war of 1947-1949<br />
ended, and the village found itself on the Jordanian<br />
side of the armistice line with its land on the<br />
Israeli side.  (That land had been slated to be part<br />
of the Arab state of Palestine in the U.N. 1947<br />
Partition Plan, but was captured by the new state of<br />
Israel in the war.)  When Israel occupied the West<br />
Bank in 1967, more land was confiscated to build<br />
illegal Israeli settlements nearby.  Now, with the<br />
construction of Israel's "Separation Fence" (which we<br />
are calling the Apartheid Wall), they are being cut<br />
off from 70% of what's left.  Farmers have one gate in<br />
the fence through which they may pass to get to their<br />
fields, but when internationals aren't present to<br />
monitor, they are often detained and/or beaten by the<br />
private armed security guards hired by the contractors<br />
who are building the fence for the Israeli government.</p>

<p>First, we attended a lecture in the municipality by a<br />
Qalqilya hydrologist about how the Oslo Agreement maps<br />
and the location of the Apartheid Wall have nothing to<br />
do with security, and everything to do with stealing<br />
access to West Bank aquifers.  (Millions of<br />
Palestinians in the West Bank have limited access to<br />
fresh water for living and for agriculture, while<br />
hundreds of thousands of Jewish settlers in the same<br />
territory have watered lawns and swimming pools.) <br />
Then the people of Jayyous, with international<br />
accompaniment, marched through the olive groves to the<br />
fence.  The women and young men of the village chanted<br />
for a while as we watched the construction equipment<br />
completing the fence that cuts through Jayyous's<br />
lands.  Some nearby soldiers watched, and some of the<br />
security guards joined them, but the men of the<br />
village made sure that the youngsters kept their<br />
distance.  On the way back, some of the boys had<br />
slingshots to throw stones at the security guards who<br />
stood menacingly in the distance as we passed, but<br />
they were not permitted by their elders to engage even<br />
in this symbolic act of violence.  The march ended<br />
without the soldiers gassing, beating, arresting or<br />
shooting at anyone, a testament to the discipline of<br />
the Jayyous organizers as well as the effect of<br />
international accompaniment.</p>

<p>Back at the municipality, the mayor thanked us for our<br />
support, and explained that the Israeli decision to<br />
cut them off from their land and therefore their<br />
income is an attempt to force them to abandon their<br />
homes.   I agree.  The fence is part of a policy of<br />
ethnic cleansing, in which parts of the West Bank are<br />
slowly being emptied of their indigenous Palestinian<br />
population and replace with Jewish settlers, many of<br />
them from the United States.</p>

<p>We proceeded from Jayyous to nearby Qalqilya, through<br />
the Azun roadblock and the Qalqilya checkpoint. <br />
Again, we made it through the checkpoint with our<br />
Israeli-American member coming under special scrutiny<br />
and pretending not to know Hebrew.  We joined up with<br />
the crew of Americans and one Brit already here.  And<br />
met our local coordinators.</p>

<p>Qalqilya is a town of about 50,000 Palestinian Muslims<br />
that sits right on the Green Line, next to the most<br />
densely populated part of Israel.  It's surrounded by<br />
rich agricultural lands, and appears to be a busy,<br />
though not particularly prosperous, trading center.  I<br />
imagine that things were much better here before the<br />
Intifada, when Israelis came here to shop and<br />
Palestinians from neighboring communities didn't have<br />
to beg soldiers to let them through checkpoints in<br />
order to come here.  When I participated in a summer<br />
high school program in Israel in 1980, we were taken<br />
to the position in Qalqilya where Jordanian artillery<br />
had been able to shell Israel before 1967.  We were<br />
taught that the occupation of the West Bank was<br />
important to protect this narrow and heavily populated<br />
strip of Israel.  We were not take to see the town of<br />
Qalqilya, not taught anything about how the people of<br />
Qaqilya suffered during fighting between Israel and<br />
Jordan 1948-1967, nor given any indication of what is<br />
was like for the people of Qalqilya to live under<br />
military occupation.  The occupation was 13 years old<br />
then.  Now it's 36.</p>

<p>Our primary local coordinator, Marwan*, invited the<br />
12 of us to his home for a delicious meal.  That<br />
evening, we were hosted at Qalqilya's Amal Society for<br />
the Deaf, the only deaf society in the Arab world<br />
that's run entirely by deaf people.  We toured their<br />
school, received dictionaries of Arabic sign language,<br />
and had a fascinating triple-translated discussion. <br />
Hamid*, a member of the Society, spoke to us in sign,<br />
the Society's one hearing member translated what he<br />
had signed into spoken Arabic, and Marwan translated<br />
from Arabic into English.  Hamid taught us a great<br />
deal about the Society and its programs and<br />
international contacts.  He also told us about the<br />
deaf Palestinians who have been maimed and killed by<br />
Israeli soldiers during this Intifada.  In each case,<br />
soldiers had opened fire on an unarmed deaf<br />
Palestinian man after he failed to follow an order to<br />
halt that he did not hear.  Hamid also told us about<br />
the night last year when Israeli soldiers raided the<br />
Society's boarding school, terrifying the children and<br />
wrecking their offices and computer lab.</p>

<p>Yesterday, we were invited to a demonstration in the<br />
town center in support of political prisoners in<br />
Israeli prisons.  Tens of thousands of Palestinian men<br />
are in prison in Israel under the administrative<br />
detention law that relieves the Israeli government of<br />
the responsibility of laying charges or trying the<br />
Palestinians the army arrests.  Abed*, my host from my<br />
first time with ISM in April 2002, was imprisoned<br />
under this law after his wedding and while his new<br />
wife was pregnant with their first child.  As far as I<br />
know, he's never met his daughter.</p>

<p>The demonstration was lively and colorful, and<br />
without army interference.  The army came in the<br />
middle of the night last week to assassinate someone<br />
(look up "extrajudicial executions" on Amnesty<br />
International's website to find out just how flagrant<br />
a human rights violation that is), but in general they<br />
don't come into Qalqilya much.  Instead, they've built<br />
a wall and a fence all the way around the town, so<br />
that the only way in or out is the checkpoint.  Now<br />
that 50,000 people are locked in a cage, there's<br />
little need for the Israeli soldiers to step into the<br />
cage with them.  One little boy in the demonstration<br />
was holding a photo of his mother, who was murdered by<br />
Israeli soldiers.</p>

<p>In the afternoon, we went to look at the wall that<br />
separates Qalqilya from Israel and from the rest of<br />
the West Bank.  A photo of the wall is attached. <br />
Construction started in April of 2002, and the<br />
Qalqilya will be completely surrounded some time this<br />
summer.  We passed by the new girls' school that was<br />
attacked with tear gas by the Israeli army last year. <br />
Now they don't gas anyone-they watch from their tower<br />
in an 8-meter-high concrete wall.</p>

<p>We also visited a farmers' road into orchards near the<br />
"security fence", which has been blocked by 4<br />
roadblocks by the Israeli army.  We're going to talk<br />
with the Farmers' Union about clearing the roadblocks<br />
together.  Last, we visited a gate in the fence meant<br />
to allow farmers to pass into their fields and<br />
orchards on the "Israeli"  side of the fence.  The<br />
fence at that point is indeed a fence, about 8 feet<br />
high.  It appears to be electrified, although the<br />
cable may be for motion sensors or camera.  On the<br />
"Israeli" side of the fence is a paved road that is<br />
restricted to army jeeps, and on the Palestinian side<br />
there is a dirt track, a ditch, and rolls and rolls of<br />
razor wire.  The path to the gate is impassable except<br />
on foot or donkey.  Farmers were returning from their<br />
fields without interference, but we're told that at<br />
times Israeli soldiers prevent them from passing<br />
through the gate.</p>

<p>This morning, four of us spent two hours watching<br />
Qalqilya Checkpoint.  There was nothing out of the<br />
ordinary-just the usual humiliations of men being<br />
forced to wait for an hour in the sun while the<br />
soldiers hold their IDs, and then being allowed to<br />
pass.  Yesterday, other members of our team intervened<br />
on behalf of a taxi driver who was arbitrarily<br />
detained and his taxi confiscated.  They succeeded in<br />
getting him his ID back and passage through the<br />
checkpoint, and will try today to get his taxi back. <br />
The cab is his only way to feed his family.</p>

<p>Everyone we've met in Qalqilya has been warmly<br />
welcoming, and we've received official invitations to<br />
events as well as lots of public thank yous.  We're<br />
being treated a little like a delegation to be feted<br />
and not as participants in non-violent resistance, but<br />
we're working toward a more active relationship with<br />
the community.  There was a big meeting today toward<br />
that end while I was at checkpoint watch, and I'm<br />
looking forward to hearing about what was<br />
accomplished.</p>

<p>That's all for now.  Thanks for reading, and call<br />
anytime.</p>

<p>Steve</p>

<p>*In these journals, I will always use pseudonyms when<br />
writing about Palestinian individuals other than<br />
members of ISM's core group.  Using their real names<br />
could potentially subject them to imprisonment by the<br />
Israeli government, which has historically taken a dim<br />
view of Palestinian non-violent organizing.</p>
