<p>I saw this "Editorial Observer" <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2006/08/31/opinion/31observer.html?n=Top%2fReference%2fTimes%20Topics%2fPeople%2fR%2fRosenthal%2c%20Andrew">piece</a> by Andrew Rosenthal in yesterday's <u>NYTimes</u> and I could hardly believe my eyes.  The writer asks, in the words of the piece's title, "There Is Silence in the Streets; Where Have All the Protesters Gone?Â”, and then goes on to complain about how indifferent to injustice, or plain soft or cowardly, today's generation is when compared to the nobility of his own:</p>

<blockquote>
<p>. . . itÂ’s hard to imagine anyone on todayÂ’s campuses willing to face armed troops. Is there anything they care about that much?</p>

<p>Student protesters helped drive Lyndon Johnson Â— in so many ways a powerful, progressive president Â— out of office because of his war. In 2004, George W. Bush Â— in so many ways a weak, regressive president Â— was re-elected despite his war. And the campuses were silent.</p>

<p>Yes, the lack of a military draft is an important explanation for the lack of political involvement on today's campuses, but Rosenthal is being more than a little disingenuous in not mentioning the most important element of what he portrays as, at best, the apathy of our youth.</p>

<p>I'm outraged that an Assistant Managing Editor of the <u>Times</u> can pretend to be blind to the fact that we <em>have</em> taken to the streets, repeatedly, in numbers of up to a million people in New York alone.   We were virtually ignored by our President, our Senators and Representatives, our courts and, most importantly, our media, notably his own employer.  Little has changed even today, when two thirds of the country opposes the Iraq War and just about everything else done by this administration.  We've also voted, tried to nominate candidates, written letters, made phone calls, leafletted, hung posters, organized action groups, started committees and blogs, created art and eventually screamed at the top of our lungs.  We've been arrested for protesting, or for looking like we were going to protest, and sometimes we've been injured or held for days without charges.  We remain nevertheless virtually invisible and even less effective in impacting Washington than, say, Miami Cubans have had in influencing Havana.   Now there's something worth thinking about, Mr. Rosenthal.</p>

<p>Fortunately some <u>Times</u> readers know a fool or a villain when they spot one, as I was pleased to learn in going through <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2006/09/02/opinion/l02protest.html?_r=1&oref=slogin">all five letters</a> on the subject of his column which appeared in today's edition. <blockquote>
<p>Andrew Rosenthal raises a timely question. The silence in the streets is partly a sign of millions of tired or retired protesters. There is also silence because we currently have an administration that would not listen to protests if 200 million Americans marched on Washington.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>And Nancy Goor is more specific:</p>

<blockquote>

<p>Or is Mr. Rosenthal referring to the streets of Washington, where hundreds of thousands of people have marched and protested since before the war? At least four major antiwar demonstrations have been held there since January 2003.</p>
