<p>I couldn't stand looking at it any more, even if it shows a <em>tweaked</em> image of the Whole Foods logo, one which now serves as the symbol of a national boycott.  I'm referring to my last, melancholy post, "<a href="/2009/08/whole_foods_more_tha.html">Whole Foods' John Mackey wants us unwholesome</a>".   So, thinking I might not be the only one so depressed, I decided to upload a picture of some flowers I snapped up on the <a href="/2009/06/the_high_line_opens.html">High Line</a> at around 4 o'clock on this warm, sleepy afternoon.  I was walking back from <a href="http://www.buonitalia.com/Default.aspx?AspxAutoDetectCookieSupport=1">Buon Italia</a> in Chelsea Market, a really, really, wonderful sort-of-smallish, genuine food emporium for anyone interested in Italian food, now destined to become an even more important part of my food shopping rounds.</p>

<p>Then, remembering how few people I saw up there today, I thought about how the beautiful slim park, which has quickly become the site of a <a href="http://video.nytimes.com/video/2009/07/24/style/1247463599601/on-the-street-elevated.html?scp=8&sq=high%20line%20&st=cse">documented</a> neighborhood <em>passeggiata</em>, manages to look very different at various hours and on various days of the week.   Here are a couple images showing just how popular it is on weekends.  Both of them were taken on the first of August, a Saturday.</p>

<p>These pictures remind me of why I decided years ago that I had to move to New York permanently:  Weekends here seemed to have been arranged mostly for visitors, and they still are, although back in the early 80's I was thinking of Downtown <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tom_Cora">music</a>, theater, dance bars and performance, which were always much more interesting on school nights.</p>
