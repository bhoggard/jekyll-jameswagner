<p>In a compact, tightly-argued <a href="Once more we are reminded that no one really owns art, that all collectors are temporary custodians. And the greater the art, the less any one person, especially a dead one, can control its destiny.">piece</a> in this morning's <u>NYTimes</u> Roberta Smith puts the brouhaha over the <a href="http://www.washingtonpost.com/ac2/wp-dyn/A135-2004Dec14?language=printer">disposition</a> of the collection of the <a href="http://www.barnesfoundation.org/">Barnes Foundation</a> into a clear perspective.</p>

<blockquote>
<p>Once more we are reminded that no one really owns art, that all collectors are temporary custodians. And the greater the art, the less any one person, especially a dead one, can control its destiny.</p>

<p>In the end, art belongs to the people it inspires, the people who use it to understand themselves and the world better - and the people who use it to create more art, and the possibility of more inspiration.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>I feel compelled to add here that my respects come from someone who really loves <a href="http://www.barnesfoundation.org/v_p_inq42003.html">old hinges</a> but hasn't been to Merion, Pennsylvania.</p>
