<p><img alt="Suzanne_Thorpe_Philip_White_Balancing_Act.jpg" src="/images/Suzanne_Thorpe_Philip_White_Balancing_Act.jpg" width="600" height="399" class="mt-image-none" /><br />
<a href="http://www.suzannethorpe.com/">Suzanne Thorpe</a> and <a href="http://www.prwhite.net/">Philip White</a> created "Balancing Act", a psychoacoustic composition which related the list of the ship to the location of the listener on the cabin deck</p>

<p><img alt="Richard_Garet_Inner_Outer.jpg" src="/images/Richard_Garet_Inner_Outer.jpg" width="600" height="399" class="mt-image-none" /><br />
<a href="http://www.richardgaret.com/">Richard Garet</a>'s "Inner-Outer" <em>harmonized</em> a video projection of the abstract, crystalline effect of light reflections bouncing on the water's surface with a sound collage of recordings made underwater</p>

<p><img alt="Clarke_Owen_Takeda_Underfoot.jpg" src="/images/Clarke_Owen_Takeda_Underfoot.jpg" width="600" height="399" class="mt-image-none" /><br />
with "Underfoot", <a href="http://www.mfclarke.com/">Melissa Clarke</a>, <a href="http://benowen.org/">Ben Owen</a> and <a href="http://www.shimpeitakeda.com/">Shimpei Takeda</a> recreated the Hudson River bed within the ship's bowels, using projections, sound, reflective materials composed of geographical data, and light</p>

<p><img alt="Bart_Bridger_Woodstrup_Gathering_Lore.jpg" src="/images/Bart_Bridger_Woodstrup_Gathering_Lore.jpg" width="600" height="399" class="mt-image-none" /><br />
<a href="http://www.bartwoodstrup.com/">Bart Bridge Woodstrup</a>'s "Gathering Lore", set up on the ship's bridge, was a weather station which translated current meteorological conditions into sound</p>

<p><img alt="Jessica_Feldman_Sirens.jpg" src="/images/Jessica_Feldman_Sirens.jpg" width="600" height="399" class="mt-image-none" /><br />
<a href="http://jessicafeldman.org/">Jessica Feldman</a>'s evocative piece, "Sirens", heard throughout the ship, and beyond, reflected the ship's original function, warning sailors, simultaneously playing with the natural seductive quality of sound</p>

<p>It's not often that I get a chance to post my own images from my experience of a musical performance.  Even if it might be better described as a musical "installation", my ears and my camera both delighted in "<a href="http://www.emfproductions.org/upcomingevents0809/fryingpan.html">Sound in the Frying Pan</a>", a remarkable project put together this past weekend by the Electronic Music Foundation in and on the "<a href="http://fryingpan.com/">Frying Pan</a>", a historic decommissioned lightship moored in the Hudson River at the end of 26th Street.   What you see above are a just a few bits from my collection of visual takes on the five separate site-specific compositions created by the artists or artist-collaboratives who worked on this quite literally "phenomenal" sound project, curated by <a href="http://www.suzannethorpe.com/">Suzanne Thorpe</a>.</p>

<p>This post, because of the images, may seem to be as much about the "Frying Pan" as is about the music, but I've been to the ship before* and yesterday it wasn't only the squeaking of its old metal plates that I heard as it rolled gently alongside the dock, although that sound accompanied the ensemble introduced both above and below its decks;  yesterday the old barnacle-encrusted veteran actually <em>sang</em>.</p>

<p>*<br />
beginning in September 2000, in the halcyon days before Bush 2, with the appearance of <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Miss_Kittin">Miss Kittin</a> in the program, "<a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2000/09/15/movies/pop-and-jazz-guide-150983.html">BATOFAR: NEW FRENCH ELECTRONICA</a>"</p>
