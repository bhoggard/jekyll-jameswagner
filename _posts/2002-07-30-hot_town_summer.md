<p>[Miss Scarlet must have been told that a lady does not sweat in the summer.  She glows.  How were us northern folk supposed to know that?  It would'a made all the difference for a lot of us, surely.]</p>

<p>The <u>NYTimes</u> editors like to regularly wax poetical about our more natural seasonal pleasures, and pains, sometimes actually adding something worthy to what we normally enjoy or suffer privately.  <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2002/07/30/opinion/30TUE4.html">Today's notes </a>are sort of a tribute to the fullness of summer in New York,</p>

<blockquote>
<p>The thickness of the weather is most obvious just at dusk, when the heat tapers off a bit but the humidity comes into its own. Darkness seems to arrive from no place in particular. It condenses into a vapor that lies low over the hills north of the city and obscures the river edge of New Jersey. The damp air blunts every outline and blurs the distinction between colors until finally only darkness is left, but a darkness you can taste in your mouth and feel on your skin.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>and yet they offer some sensible help in dealing with its discomforts.</p>

<blockquote>
<p>Southerners know that the trick of living in damp heat is getting used to being sticky all the time. Northerners prefer to feel as though they'd just dried off after a long shower, their skin slick, except that in weather like this that effect quickly vanishes. For all the clarity of azure autumn days and the softness of the best weeks of spring, a few days of shirt-drenching weather in late July or early August are every bit as truly New York.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>[I guess winter just doesn't count anymore.  I think we did away with it a little while back.]</p>
