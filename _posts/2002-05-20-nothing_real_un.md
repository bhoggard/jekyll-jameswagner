<p>Sent down from Smith because her educated father thought too much education would make her unmarriageable, over sixty years later she returned to the school she had loved so much, <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2002/05/20/education/20SMIT.html">completing her degree </a>this spring, at 87.  In the meantime she had been through two unhappy marriages and had built an impressive career.</p>

<blockquote>
<p>Ms. Martindell is mindful that the same career-versus-children concerns that led her to leave Smith the first time still echo in this generation. "I think women can have it all," she said. "We live so long, you can have the family and then have the career. I didn't do anything real until I was 50."</p>
</blockquote>

<blockquote>
<p>"A friend told me that after I graduated, I should take a year off to find myself," she reported, delightedly. "But as long as my health holds out, I need a project."</p>
</blockquote>
