<p>There are few issues more important to our own survival and that of the entire world than the state of Israel and the war in Iraq.  In two consecutive issues this month <a href="http://www.thenation.com/"><u>The</u> <u>Nation</u></a>'s contributors offer enlightenment in these areas to even the most knowledgeable reader.</p>

<p>I usually skip the many articles which only reflect what I already know or suspect, but I couldn't do without those which highlight this magazine's ability to reliably report or sensibly argue what what I'm unlikely to find anywhere else.  These two fill that description in spades.</p>

<p>Unfortunately only one of these two particular reads are available on line, but you're depriving yourself, <u>The</u> <u>Nation</u>, and <em>the nation</em> if you aren't already a subscriber.</p>

<p>An excerpt from Tom Engelhardt's"<a href="http://www.thenation.com/docprem.mhtml?i=20060327&s=engelhardt">Can You Say 'Permanent Bases'?</a>", which is not on line:</p>

<blockquote>
<p>To this day, those Little Americas [at least four "super-bases"] remain at the secret heart of "reconstruction" policy in Iraq.  As long as [Halliburton] keeps building them, there can be no genuine withdrawal.  Despite recent press visits, our super-bases remain in policy silence.  The Bush Administration does not discuss them (other than to deny their permanence).  No plans for them are debated in Congress.  The opposition Democrats generally ignore them.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>An excerpt from Philip Weiss's "<a href="http://www.thenation.com/doc/20060403/weiss">Why These Tickets are Too Hot for New York</a>", which is available on the magazine's website:</p>

<blockquote>
<p>As George Hunka, author of the theater blog Superfluities, says [about New York Theatre Workshop's cancellation of the play, "My Name is Rachel Corrie"], "This is far too important an issue for everyone to paper it over again, with everyone shaking hands for a New York Times photographer. It's an extraordinarily rare picture of the ways that New York cultural institutions make their decisions about what to produce."</p>

<p>Hunka doesn't use the J-word. Jen Marlowe does. A Jewish activist with Rachelswords.org (which is staging a reading of Corrie's words on March 22 with the Corrie parents present), she says, "I don't want to say the Jewish community is monolithic. It isn't. But among many American Jews who are very progressive and fight deeply for many social justice issues, there's a knee-jerk reflexive reaction that happens around issues related to Israel."</p>
</blockquote>
