<p>We absolutely <a href="http://bloggy.com/mt/archives/004492.html">did</a> get out to the Queens Museum of Art (QMA) last Sunday for the opening of the <a href="http://www.queensmuseum.org/exhibitions/qi2004.htm">Queens International 2004</a>.  It was almost five when we got there, so we were pretty busy for the next hour.  Most everyone in the very interesting crowd was pretty laid back, so we must have looked pretty intense as we made our way through galleries showing the art of some 50 or more Queens-based artists.  Even so we managed to talk to a number of them while they hung out near their work.</p>

<p>There is nothing of the provinces, the "outer boroughs," about this show - except maybe for the incredible ethnic diversity of the artists included - a heterogeneity which frequently shows up even in the compound heritage of one individual.  The name of the show, "Queens International," is a salute to that diversity.  Any city in the country would be proud of the quality of the art represented.  It's a first-rate show, a first-rate New York show.</p>

<p>Some of my favorites:</p>

<blockquote>
<p><a href="http://www.re-title.com/artists/haeri-yoo.asp">Haeri Yoo</a>'s wall of childlike drawings which almost mask her sophisticated humanism</p>

<p><a href="http://www.findarticles.com/p/articles/mi_m1248/is_10_91/ai_109667953">Cui Fei</a>'s enormous, but so delicate, sculptural evocation of Chinese caligraphy employing grape vine tendrils</p>

<p><a href="http://www.wendycoopergallery.com/chrisdorland.htm">Chris Dorland</a>'s use of World's Fair pavilions to comment in his paintings on our utopian dreams - and follies</p>

<p><a href="http://www.aissadeebi.8k.com/">Aissa Deebi</a>'s photographic documentary of exile, using a shisha cafe in Astoria as his canvas</p>

<p><a href="http://www.bellwethergallery.com/artistsindex_01.cfm?fid=12">Matt Ducklo</a>'s sort-of-photojounalist suburban grotesques (especially the Kentucky shopping center/cemetary/mountain range combo)</p>

<p>Pascal Jalabert's heroic paper-tape bridges in electric colors</p>

<p><a href="http://www.clementine-gallery.com/kl/01.html">Kurt Lightner</a>'s magical mylar cut-out collages (and ink drawing)</p>

<p><a href="http://www.keith-miller.com/curatorial/threadbare/nava.html">Nava Lubelski</a>'s abstract canvases, which she stains and then adorns with needlepoint to almost electric effect</p>

<p><a href="http://www.grandarts.com/exhibits/TRichards.html">Troy Richards</a>'s bold candy windows (see above)</p>

<p><a href="http://www.deeplistening.org/dlc/120howard.html">Earl Howard</a>'s sound sculpture installed in the Whitney-size elevator</p>

<p><a href="http://www.smackmellon.org/studioart/kim.html">Shin Il Kim</a>'s animated video whimsical profundities</p>

<p>Minshik shin's totally wonderful and sincere "American Dream" video</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Because of the crowds and because of our limited time, we missed our chance to see most of the video art, so the list above is even more imperfect than usual.</p>
