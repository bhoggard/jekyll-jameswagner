<p>I'd heard about a bit about her before, but when I arrived at page 38 of today's <u>Newsday</u> and saw that beautiful face turned to the camera the name Harriet Quimby somehow came to life for the first time.    It was an illustration for the paper's regular half-page <a href="http://www.newsday.com/other/special/ny-iholi0828story,0,5944356.htmlstory">feature</a>, "IT HAPPENED ON LONG ISLAND".  This morning it carried the headline, "1911: America's First Licensed Woman Pilot".   The picture shows Quimby seated inside her Moisant monoplane*, probably the one on which she learned to fly.</p>

<p>Her life makes a terrific <a href="http://www.uh.edu/engines/epi1696.htm">story</a>, and while it wasn't to last very long that bright face still winks at us today.</p>

<p>I did a quick Google search to find more images of Quimby and this is the one which really inspired this post:</p>

<p><img alt="Quimby_propeller.jpg" src="/mt_archives/Quimby_propeller.jpg" width="450" height="637" /></p>

<p>The shot may be a bit fuzzy but almost a hundred years after her death it shows that the woman who always wore her self-designed plum-colored, satin flying suit (the pant legs converted into a walking skirt) when she was anywhere near a plane, was much more than a pretty <em>face</em>.</p>

<p>Finally, a breathtaking image of Quimby and her 50-horsepower Moisant in flight, to suggest the thrill , the danger and, yes, the sometime beauty and gracefulness of air travel in 1912:</p>

<p><img alt="Quimby_in_flight.jpg" src="/mt_archives/Quimby_in_flight.jpg" width="575" height="323" /></p>

<p>*<br />
It appears that the paper has it wrong, describing the plane as a Bleriot XI;  the Moisant was actually designed and built by the <a href="http://www.commercemarketplace.com/home/CollectAir/moisantindex.html">Moisant bothers</a>, aviation pioneers along with their sister Matilde at Hempstead on Long Island, Newsday's backyard.</p>
