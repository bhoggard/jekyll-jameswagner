Saori, the animated host of a children's cartoon program, lives in a bubble</p>

<p>Wow.  We don't often get a second chance to experience one of John Moran's creations, but this week Performance Space 122 is hosting a return of "Saori's Birthday!" as part of its <a href="http://www.ps122.org/performances/coil_2008.html">COIL</a> festival.</p>

<p>You don't have just <a href="/2007/10/john_moran_and.html">my word</a> for its virtues, since the <a href="http://query.nytimes.com/gst/fullpage.html?res=9E02E5D7173AF930A15751C1A9619C8B63&scp=1&sq=gia+kourlas+john+moran">Gia Kourlas</a>, the <u>NYTimes</u> critic, included it among her personal list of the most important new works of the year, and last October <a href="http://www.villagevoice.com/theater/0744,soloski,78203,11.html">Alexis Soloski</a> of <u>The</u> <u>Voice</u> wound up her review with these lines:</p>

<blockquote>
<p>A brief confection, lasting barely 40 minutes, What If Saori Had a Party? continues Moran's recent trend away from his large-scale operas of the '90sÂ—performed at venues such as Lincoln Center and A.R.T.Â—and toward smaller, more intimate works. It also continues his longtime interest in representing characters not quite human or not quite whole; other works have concerned robots, child-men, and Jack Benny. And it marks another collaborationÂ—likely not the lastÂ—with the luminous Tsukada. As she slides about her airless space, registering every pre-recorded shriek, shudder, whistle, and thump, she's profoundly silly. Tsukada's gyrations, Moran's thorny score, and the air of candy-colored dreadÂ—what a swell party this is.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Performances of this twenty-first-century dance/music/theater gem continue today through Sunday.  Tickets can be reserved on the PS 122 <a href="http://www.ps122.org/performances/what_if_saori_had_a_party.html">site</a>.  They're pretty cheap, but they're also just about priceless.</p>

<p>Barry and I hope to see it again on Saturday.</p>
