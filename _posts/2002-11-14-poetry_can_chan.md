<p><a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2002/11/12/nyregion/12PROF.html">Bob Holman thinks so</a>, and it seems like his <a href="">Bowery Poetry Club</a> may have a good chance to do just that.  It looks and sounds wonderful, but the genie himself may be what makes the difference.</p>

<blockquote>
<p>This is the poet, a former cabdriver and temporary worker, who used to call himself Plain White Rapper. For a few years, he ran a spoken-word record label, Mouth Almighty.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Last month he opened this, er, club.</p>

<blockquote>
<p>"I run a coffee shop and bar so you can have poetry every night," he said. "Somehow, you have to pay for your addiction. They say no one has ever gone broke running a bar in New York, but we're going to give it a shot."</p>
</blockquote>

<p><a href="http://www.villagevoice.com/issues/0243/carr.php">Taylor Mead</a>, <a href="http://www.ps1.org/cut/volume/morris.html">Butch Morris</a>, <a href="http://www.observer.com/pages/story.asp?ID=6465">Amiri Baraka</a>, teen poetry slams, karaoke poetry, <a href="http://www.sayheykey.de/">Norman Ohler</a>, <a href="http://www.avantart.com/music/nedro.htm">Ned Rothenberg</a> and <a href="http://www.dirtybasement.com/">Uncle Jimmy's Dirty Basement</a>, are among the starters this month.</p>

<p>The club shares the building with the intriguing, <a href="http://www.dvdojo.com/">DV Dojo</a>, "a boot camp for digital filmmakers," in Holman's description.  Last night we also noticed it's just across the street from the <a href="http://www.cbgb.com/gallery.html">313 Gallery</a> of <a href="http://www.cbgb.com/">CBGB</a>, where we had stopped for the opening of the provocative and still largely illegal work in the exhibition, <a href="http://www.illegal-art.org/">Illegal Art</a>, especially to see <a href="http://www.ericdoeringer.com/">Eric Doeringer</a>'s installation.</p>

<p>It all seems more than fitting, if not world-changing.</p>
