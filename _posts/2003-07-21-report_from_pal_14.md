<p><a href="https://jameswagner.com/index.php?m=200307#955">Steve</a> writes from Jenin.</p>

<blockquote>
<p>Jenin, West Bank, Occupied Palestine<br />
Sunday, July 20, 2003</p>

<p>On Wednesday evening in Qalqilya, we ISM folks were<br />
invited to meet with representatives of the<br />
organizations that comprise the PLO in Qalqilya.  They<br />
were all middle-aged men, and all had done time in<br />
Israeli prisons (as has Marwan, our local coordinator,<br />
as have most Palestinian men in the occupied<br />
territories).  Each of them spoke about the misery of<br />
occupation, the falseness of Israel's peace<br />
negotiations, and the Palestinian determination to<br />
resist.  We threw out a few ideas about direct action<br />
that we can participate in alongside the community,<br />
and there will be more meetings to knock around some<br />
ideas.</p>

<p>The meeting was followed immediately by a second<br />
meeting, with representatives of the farmers' union. <br />
We spoke about the roadblocks on the road to orchards<br />
within the fence, difficulty in access to their land<br />
outside the fence, irrigation lines being cut by the<br />
workers constructing the fence, and so on.  I thought<br />
about the day last fall when Lysander and other ISM<br />
folks were asked by the farmers to join them in<br />
witnessing the destruction of their fruit trees to<br />
clear a path for the fence.  She described how some of<br />
the farmers cried and had to be led away.</p>

<p>We decided that we will go out into the fields and the<br />
orchards with the farmers on Sunday to work alongside<br />
them and to witness the difficulties they encounter. <br />
Then we'll sit with them that evening to decide what<br />
needs to be done in Qalqilya.</p>

<p>In a third meeting on Wednesday night (oy), this time<br />
just ISM, we decided who would replace the interim ISM<br />
international coordinator in Qalqilya, since she's<br />
leaving this weekend.  Lysander and I volunteered to<br />
share the role.</p>

<p>Thursday morning, we returned to court in Tel Aviv for<br />
the deportation hearing of the 8 ISM internationals<br />
arrested in Jenin and Nablus.  They had 4 of the top<br />
human-rights lawyers in Israel, and a packed court of<br />
international and Israeli supporters.  The court<br />
officers kept many of the supporters in the hallway<br />
throughout the proceeding, even though there were<br />
empty seats in the courtroom.</p>

<p>Our lawyers pointed out that the 2 Israelis arrested<br />
with the 8 internationals were released almost<br />
immediately, that the arrests were illegal, that the<br />
facts alleged were contradictory.  They produced<br />
affidavits in support of ISM from Member of Knesset<br />
Yossi Sarid and from Terri Greenblatt of Bat Shalom. <br />
They showed that while the Ministry of the Interior<br />
was alleging that ISM interferes with the activities<br />
of the army, endangering themselves, soldiers, and the<br />
Israeli public, they offered no evidence to show that<br />
the 8 defendants interfered with the army in any way.</p>

<p>The judge upheld the Ministry's deportation order<br />
anyway, and agreed with the Ministry's<br />
characterizations of ISM.  He also denied a one-week<br />
stay of deportation while an appeal is filed in the<br />
Israeli Supreme Court.</p>

<p>We spent last night in Jerusalem.   The pedestrian<br />
mall in West Jerusalem was packed, because it was<br />
Thursday night (everything's closed Friday night for<br />
the Jewish Sabbath), and because there is a currently<br />
a cease fire between the Israeli army and Hamas,<br />
Islamic Jihad and the Al-Aqsa Martyrs' Brigade. <br />
Everyone entering the outdoor mall had to be<br />
thoroughly checked by one of a legion of security<br />
guards.  I found it pretty scary.  I also thought that<br />
the Israeli peace movement ought to do an action<br />
there, hanging banners on the barricades that point<br />
out that it's the Occupation that makes metal<br />
detectors on a city street necessary.</p>

<p>I was with Lisa, from JAtO, who doesn't read Hebrew,<br />
so I was translating the graffiti and political<br />
posters on the walls for her.  They were uniformly<br />
right wing, and said things like "Kahane was right",<br />
"Jordan is the Palestinian state", and "Oslo proves:<br />
it's forbidden to give them a state."  There was even<br />
graffiti on the walls of the Old City.  (To be fair,<br />
there's lots of graffiti in Palestinian communities<br />
throughout the West Bank, and I usually can't read<br />
what it says.)</p>

<p>The previous week when I was in West Jerusalem, I saw<br />
a number of young men who appeared to be Arab pulled<br />
aside by police, apparently based on looks alone, to<br />
have their IDs scrutinized and to be questioned about<br />
their activities.</p>

<p>While in Jerusalem, I got a call from the ISM people<br />
who had returned to Qalqilya from Tel Aviv.  They were<br />
absolutely denied entry to Qalqilya via the<br />
checkpoint; apparently, the Israeli army wants the<br />
50,000 people of Qalqilya, entirely surrounded by the<br />
wall/fence, to be cut off from the outside world.  Our<br />
people ended up crawling under a locked farmers' gate<br />
in a part of the fence away from the checkpoint, in<br />
the dark.  It remains to be seen how this will play<br />
out, but it looks like our mobility in and out of<br />
Qalqilya is going to be very limited.</p>

<p>Friday morning, 3 of us from the Qalqilya crew<br />
traveled from Jerusalem to Jenin to help out with an<br />
action.  Getting from Jerusalem to Jenin was a 45<br />
minute drive once upon a time, but now that a network<br />
of settler roads has been built in the West Bank and<br />
declared off limits to vehicles with Palestinian<br />
license plates (while West Bank cities are off limits<br />
to vehicles with Israeli license plates), the trip<br />
involves a long detour through the Jordan Valley, many<br />
humiliating checkpoints, and 3 hours' travel time. <br />
One of the passengers in our van was a young man from<br />
Jerusalem who is a student at the Arab-American<br />
University in Zababde, a village near Jenin.  His<br />
Jerusalem ID means he is seen as an Israeli by the<br />
authorities, so each week when he goes to school, he<br />
gets stopped at the last checkpoint and told that he<br />
mustn't go to Jenin "for his own safety".  The delay<br />
caused by the soldiers checking his ID led the driver<br />
to leave without him, stranding him at the checkpoint.</p>

<p>We got to Birqin, near Jenin, just in time to<br />
participate in a <b>roadblock removal</b>*.  Lots of men and<br />
boys from the village, as well as the ISM crew from<br />
Jenin, converged on the giant dirt mound with a front<br />
loader, pick axes, and shovels.  If you look carefully<br />
at the attached photo, you'll see two people hanging<br />
off the sides of the front loader.  Those are ISM<br />
internationals there to protect the front loader from<br />
confiscation, and the driver from arrest.  My job was<br />
to eavesdrop on the soldiers communicating with one<br />
another, since I understand Hebrew, while another<br />
international negotiated with them in English. <br />
Fortunately, I had nothing to do, since the army never<br />
showed up.  The roadblock that the army built is gone,<br />
and the drive from Birqin to Jenin is once again 5<br />
minutes, instead of 40.</p>

<p>After the successful action, we spent time at the home<br />
of Moayed, an organizer in Birqin.  We were served tea<br />
and coffee, of course, and listened to Moayed and his<br />
family play the oud and sing songs of Palestinian<br />
liberation.  His teenage daughter recited a poem about<br />
Palestine that made a Palestinian-American ISM member<br />
cry.  It was great chatting with Moayed; he spoke with<br />
me about the need for coexistence of Jews and<br />
Palestinians in this land, and about how the Torah and<br />
the Qur'an are both used to justify exclusive rights<br />
to the country.</p>

<p>We proceeded to the ISM apartment in Jenin.  The walls<br />
of Jenin are covered with martyr posters (anyone who<br />
has died in the struggle is called a "martyr" in<br />
Palestine), from Rachel Corrie to civilians shot by<br />
Israeli soldiers in Jenin to fighters who died<br />
defending Jenin from Israeli invasion to suicide<br />
bombers.  One sees these posters in every Palestinian<br />
community in the West Bank and the Gaza Strip, but<br />
they're particularly plentiful here.  I am sorry that<br />
bombers get the same status as other people who resist<br />
the Occupation; personally, I'm convinced that the<br />
bombings are reprehensible as well as<br />
counterproductive.  I worry about them when I'm in Tel<br />
Aviv, Haifa, or West Jerusalem (I never ride buses),<br />
and my friend Shanka in Tel Aviv narrowly missed<br />
getting killed in a bus bombing a year ago.  I think<br />
it's important to remember, however, that there were<br />
almost no bombings when the peace process was on track<br />
in the '90s, that the bloody Israeli army assault on<br />
unarmed Palestinian resistance in September, October<br />
and November of 2000 preceded any of this Intifada's<br />
bombings, and that Israeli army targeting of<br />
Palestinian civilians has killed 3 times as many<br />
people as the bombings have.  So while I disagree (to<br />
put it mildly) with anyone who sees the bombers as<br />
people to be admired for sacrificing themselves for<br />
their people, I think it's clear that the way to end<br />
the bombings is to end the Occupation.  (The Israeli<br />
Knesset this past week reaffirmed that the West<br />
Bank-"Judea and Samaria" in their Biblical view-is not<br />
occupied territory, that settlement expansion must<br />
continue, and that Israel must control all the land<br />
west of the "security fence", even though that land is<br />
in the West Bank and represents vital Palestinian land<br />
and water resources.)</p>

<p>The ISM folks in Jenin tell me that the Israeli army<br />
has been going into Jenin Refugee Camp at night,<br />
destroying the building materials that the U.N. is<br />
using to try and rebuild the community that was<br />
bulldozed by the army in April of 2002.</p>

<p>I had a good discussion tonight with folks in Jenin<br />
about their upcoming actions in and around the city,<br />
and how we might proceed in Qalqilya.  The conditions<br />
in walled-in Qalqilya are very difficult for people<br />
who live and work there, and for internationals trying<br />
to support non-violent resistance there.  The people<br />
there have welcomed internationals in solidarity with<br />
them, but I think we all feel a little stymied by<br />
being caged up.  We'll see what we can accomplish.</p>

<p>The trip Saturday morning from Jenin to Qalqilya was<br />
another exercise in roadblocks, humiliating<br />
checkpoints, and 5 shared taxis for what should have<br />
been 1 short trip.  The racism at the checkpoints was<br />
blatant; at one point all the Palestinian men in the<br />
car were forced to get out and stand in the sun while<br />
their IDs were checked.  I was allowed to sit in the<br />
car with the women.  No soldier asked spoke to me or<br />
looked at my passport to ascertain who I was; I was<br />
apparently judged not in need of checking by virtue of<br />
my appearance alone.</p>

<p>We finally got to Qalqilya, and did manage to talk our<br />
way in through the checkpoint.  We had a few things<br />
going for us: we were a small group (only 3), we had a<br />
Palestinian-American with us who could claim to have<br />
family in Qalqilya, and the District Commanding<br />
Officer who has ordered internationals kept out of<br />
Qalqilya wasn't there because it was Saturday. <br />
Nevertheless, we got in by the skin of our teeth.</p>

<p>While we waited and haggled at the checkpoint, I<br />
observed the soldiers' interactions with Palestinians<br />
requesting permission into the city.  They were spoken<br />
to and manhandled in a way that the soldiers would<br />
never dare with us, another manifestation of racism<br />
run amok.  The soldier with whom we were negotiating,<br />
who was friendly to us and sympathetic, left for a<br />
moment, transformed from Dr. Jekyll to Mr. Hyde. Hyde,<br />
and screamed at some boys on a donkey cart.  Another<br />
soldier went through a young man's pockets without<br />
speaking to him about it first, in order to see if he<br />
had another form of ID.  I couldn't imagine him doing<br />
that to me.</p>

<p>On Sunday morning we went out to the farmers' gate in<br />
the fence.  The idea was to spend a day with farmers<br />
working in their fields and orchards and observing the<br />
ways in which the fence is disrupting their<br />
livelihood.  Agriculture has become a central source<br />
of income since Palestinians' travel to their jobs in<br />
Israel was banned, and since Israeli shoppers stopped<br />
coming to Qalqilya.</p>

<p>One can no longer bring a car, truck or tractor into<br />
the Qalqilya fields and orchards outside of the fence.<br />
 The army blocked the way to the gate with boulders<br />
and a mound of dirt, so that one can only travel on<br />
foot or, with difficulty, by donkey.  The impact of<br />
this demechanization on Qalqilya farmers' ability to<br />
extract income from their fields is obvious.</p>

<p>We walked through the gate with Shukri, an AP<br />
photographer who is a Qalqilya resident and some<br />
farmers.  We were stopped by the private armed<br />
security (from a company called Ari) who work for the<br />
companies contracted to build the fence for the<br />
Israeli government.  They were very aggressive and<br />
caused all the farmers except one to turn back and try<br />
again later.  We ignored them and walked into the<br />
lands beyond the fence with a farmer named Khaled.</p>

<p>Khaled pointed out how many of the plots were<br />
neglected since September 2002 when this part of the<br />
fence went up.  Under the Ottoman land laws, which<br />
Israel uses to confiscate Palestinian land, property<br />
belongs to the state if it is uncultivated for 3 years<br />
in a row.  The state's role in preventing cultivation<br />
is not a mitigating factor in the eyes of the Israeli<br />
legal system.  The Israeli government then turns the<br />
land over to the Jewish National Fund, whose charter<br />
says that the land is held in perpetuity for the<br />
Jewish people, making it technically illegal for<br />
non-Jews, even non-Jewish Israelis, to rent or live on<br />
that land.  (The heavily fortified Border Police post<br />
at the Qalqilya checkpoint has a sign denoting that<br />
it's on JNF land.  Not what I had in mind when I put<br />
my allowance in those little blue boxes as a kid.)</p>

<p>Israeli soldiers in a Hummer followed us up the path<br />
among the fields, and forced us to leave.  We tried to<br />
negotiate to let us stay and work with the farmers for<br />
the day, but they said they were calling the Border<br />
Police to come and arrest us.  Again: apartheid.  They<br />
said that the farmer could proceed to his fields (his<br />
wife and children already had), but they were intent<br />
on keeping us apart from them.</p>

<p>One of the soldiers freaked when we walked back<br />
through the gate into Qalqilya.  I guess they thought<br />
we'd walk alongside the gate on their jeep road until<br />
we got to a checkpoint, or until the Border Police<br />
came along and arrested us.  They REALLY don't want us<br />
in Qalqilya.  They didn't follow us in, however. I<br />
think they need fairly high level orders to come<br />
inside the cage.  They did stop Shukri, and took his<br />
ID and press pass (Palestinians can be arrested for<br />
not carrying ID).  Shukri went to the District<br />
Commanding Officer later, who returned his ID, but<br />
said he'd need the name of the soldier in order to<br />
file a complaint aimed at getting back his press pass.</p>

<p>This morning we tried again to go out with the farmers<br />
(they hadn't expected that we'd come back).  We<br />
arrived at the gate at 6:15 on the assumption that the<br />
workers constructing the fence wouldn't be at work<br />
yet, and therefore security wouldn't have arrived. <br />
What we found was a tank, a jeep, and some soldiers,<br />
waiting apparently for us.  Some farmers got there at<br />
the same time, and were allowed through by the<br />
soldiers.  We of course did not attempt to cross, and<br />
I'm really disappointed that the army has so far been<br />
successful at separating us from the farmers.</p>

<p>Israeli army jeeps came into Qalqilya today and<br />
arrested someone-I don't know the details. <br />
International activists and local residents in the<br />
nearby village of Jayyous had an action today at which<br />
they went to the fence and threw food and supplies<br />
over to a Bedouin family trapped by the fence and<br />
unable to reach Jayyous themselves.</p>

<p>We're working hard on our upcoming wall actions-July<br />
28 in Jenin, July 29 in Tulkarm, July 30 here, and<br />
July 31 in Mas'ha.  We have to find a way to bring the<br />
world's attention to the fence and what it's doing to<br />
Palestinians.</p>

<p>That's all for now.  Peace.</p>

<p>Steve</p>
