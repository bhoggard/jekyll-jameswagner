<p>For starters, he's more <a href="http://www.newsday.com/news/columnists/ny-nybres213098042jan21,0,3069888.column?coll=ny%2Dnews%2Dcolumnists">interesting</a> and more his own man and woman than any of the others--and, not incidently, he's the only one who has actually served time when he was arrested.  Oh, and he's the only presidential candidate who ever showed up at City Hall for an AIDS demonstration, bless him.</p>

<p>Jimmy Breslin in today's <u>Newsday</u>:</p>

<blockquote>
<p>Take some of these Democratic candidates we've had: Mike Dukakis, Dick Gephardt, Joe Lieberman, Walter Mondale, Al Gore, and put them in a room and you'd open the window and jump out.</p>

<p>Sharpton may be stale in New York. But he is new practically everywhere else.</p>

<p>When crowds find that Sharpton can be exciting, and that he produces laughter with quick observations, he will have his moments as a candidate. He can use the language with more speed and fervor than anybody around. He is a master at "out of the past we see the future" phrases. About Martin Luther King, he told the crowd yesterday, "Celebrate the past. Fight for the future."</p>
</blockquote>

<p>He has credentials on real issues which should be the envy of any candidate--if they actually had any real interest in real issues.</p>

<blockquote>
<p>He also knows more in five minutes about hospitals, schools, ambulance responses, prison sentences for the poor, welfare, food stamps and going into the service to fight wars than the rest of these presidential candidates have learned in their lives.</p>

<p>I don't know how far he goes. But at the start, he will have some of them on the verge of throwing up after appearing with him.</p>
</blockquote>
