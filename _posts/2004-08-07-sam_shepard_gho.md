<p>We're home!</p>

<p>Since we only had a dial-up connection in the hotel room, I didn't try to post everything I wanted to while we were in Los Angeles.  This item, and perhaps a few others to follow, will make up for some of this blog's relative "silence" of the past week.</p>

<p>We wanted to get out of the man-made environment for a day and get into the desert, so we left Wilshire Boulevard and drove northeast, eventually passing  through the current greater-L.A. frontier around its <a href="http://www.economist.com/surveys/displayStory.cfm?Story_id=2609369">fabled Victorville</a>, soon after making a pit stop in the newly-created settlement of Summit Heights (where the mall was complete even if the tract homes were not).  Beyond this new asphalt camp the (unpeopled) wilderness began.  Going through open country baking in 105-degree heat (convertible top up, AC cranked), we turned back after Barstow, but well before approaching Needles, and drove back across the top of Edwards Air Force Base.</p>

<p>We had seen real and re-created ghost towns, but the two images below are descriptions of a special case, the community of <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boron,_California">Boron</a>, which <a href="http://bloggy.com/mt/">Barry</a> called a "<a href="http://www.kirjasto.sci.fi/sshepard.htm">Sam Shepard</a> town."  We both thought it was pretty cool, if a bit of a commute from Manhattan.  A special touch was provided by the excellent 80's rock coming from the white-painted repair garage with the "...troom" sign (the chamber indicated was of course the reason we had ended up there for this serendipity).</p>

<p><b>Historical note:  The 20 Mule Team Rd. marker refers to the fact that this street was once the route, established in the 1880's, of the <a href="http://www.rnrs.com/20MuleTeam/Information.htm">20 mule teams</a> which hauled borax from the borax Works in Death Valley and Amargosa to the railhead at Mojave (30 miles west of Boron).</b></p>
