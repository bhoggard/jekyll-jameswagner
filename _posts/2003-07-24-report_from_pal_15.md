<p><a href="https://jameswagner.com/index.php?m=200307#955">Steve</a> has been characteristically busy, but he writes home:</p>

<blockquote>
<p>Qalqilya, Occupied Palestine<br />
Thursday, July 24, 2003</p>

<p>On Monday night we learned that a time bomb had been<br />
found by security near the farmers' gate, and was<br />
detonated by the Israeli army.  As a result, the area<br />
around the gate was closed by the army and was<br />
crawling with troops.  We decided not to attempt<br />
access to the lands west of the fence on Tuesday. <br />
There has been speculation here that the Israeli army<br />
planted the time bomb in order to justify widening<br />
their off-limits zone on either side of the fence.</p>

<p>Tuesday morning, we participated in a demonstration<br />
organized by all the political parties in Qalqilya in<br />
support of Palestinian political prisoners in Israeli<br />
prisons.  We started with a short talk at the Qalqilya<br />
branch of the Palestine Prisoners' Association, a very<br />
important group made up of former prisoners (i.e. just<br />
about any Palestinian man) which provides support for<br />
prisoners and for their families.  They explained to<br />
us that their current focus is to have 3 prisons<br />
located on army bases (Howwara, Salem, and I can't<br />
remember the third) closed because the conditions<br />
there are so harsh as to violate not only<br />
international law, but Israeli law as well.</p>

<p>The demonstration was in the Qalqilya demonstration<br />
style: loud, colorful, and short.  ISM was there with<br />
banners and signs (a picture of us made it into the<br />
Palestinian daily Al-Ayyam today), and we're told that<br />
people were really happy to see us there.  There was a<br />
group of boys in front of us-little boys, not<br />
teenagers-who were chanting energetically without<br />
apparent adult guidance.  I was struck by how these<br />
boys see themselves as empowered members of the<br />
resistance to Israeli occupation and injustice.</p>

<p>There were family members of prisoners at the demo<br />
carrying photos of their imprisoned loved ones-some of<br />
the people carrying photos were little kids.</p>

<p>The demo went from the city circle to the office of<br />
the International Committee of the Red Cross/Geneva,<br />
where Lysander and I joined the officers of the<br />
various political parties and the head of the<br />
Prisoners' Association to present letters to the ICRC<br />
with our concerns.  The ICRC representative was an<br />
Australian who could only talk about ICRC policy,<br />
passing on concerns to the Jerusalem and Tel Aviv<br />
offices, etc.  The official meeting was interrupted by<br />
some women who demanded to know why they were being<br />
denied permits to visit their sons, and wanted to know<br />
what ICRC was going to do about it.  One has two sons<br />
in prison, and had been denied a permit as a "security<br />
risk".  She wanted to know how she, an old woman,<br />
could be a security risk?  Another also had two sons<br />
in prison, had been granted a permit to visit one, and<br />
was deemed a security risk when she applied for a<br />
permit to visit the other.  She wanted to know how she<br />
could be eligible for a permit for one visit, yet a<br />
security risk for the other.  They represented Israeli<br />
policies about family visitation as cruel and<br />
arbitrary, and expressed frustration at the ICRC's<br />
apparent impotence.</p>

<p>In the afternoon, we met with Faris, the local<br />
coordinator for a village called Mas'ha, and 5 from<br />
our action group (3 from JAtO) volunteered to go there<br />
for a couple of days.  Mas'ha has hosted a peace<br />
encampment along the fence for months now, and it has<br />
become a place for Israelis, Palestinians and<br />
internationals from all walks of life to come together<br />
in dialogue and in opposition to the fence.  The 5<br />
return tomorrow, and I look forward to hearing more<br />
about Mas'ha Camp.</p>

<p>I was talking with a little boy in front of our<br />
building, and one of the adults pointed out to me that<br />
his father was killed by the Israeli army.  There are<br />
6 children in the family.</p>

<p>Late in the evening, some local Muslim leaders came<br />
over to talk with us about Islam.  They are people who<br />
dedicate their lives to the service of Allah and the<br />
duty to be a good person, and I was thinking about how<br />
painful it must be for them to hear Islam slandered by<br />
political and religious leaders in the U.S., Israel,<br />
and elsewhere.  Their talk was a little too much like<br />
a visit from the Jehovah's Witnesses for my taste, but<br />
they were well-intentioned, and we had a good time<br />
just shooting the breeze after they were done with<br />
their spiel.</p>

<p>I was awakened at 3:00 yesterday morning by Jihad, a<br />
young man who spends time with us internationals.  He<br />
was alarmed that Israeli army jeeps had entered the<br />
city, and a couple of internationals walked him home. <br />
We then bolted our door, and I didn't sleep very well<br />
as I waited for the alarm to ring at 5:00.  At 6:00 <br />
[the hours seem to be accidently transposed in these <br />
few lines - JAW]   I saw a jeep driving right near our <br />
apartment, and quickly ducked inside.</p>

<p>I was up at 5:00 for attempt #3 to go out with the<br />
farmers - successful this time!!  There were no <br />
soldiers or security at the farmers' gate, and we high<br />
-tailed it into the fields west of the fence.  We <br />
ducked behind some trees as construction vehicles and<br />
security sped past, and were not spotted.</p>

<p>We were horrified, however, to see that the Israeli<br />
army had dug a trench between the gate and the road<br />
from Qalqilya, and piled the dirt and boulders up<br />
before the trench.  Passage into the lands outside of<br />
the fence, impossible by car, truck, or tractor for<br />
months, is now impassable by donkey as well.  Farmers<br />
must bring in their crops on foot.  Some of the trees<br />
immediately west of the fence and its attendant jeep<br />
road had been destroyed by a tank or a bulldozer.</p>

<p>Mohammad from the Peasants' Union took us around the<br />
lands of Qalqilya and Jayyous all morning.  We stopped<br />
and talked with many farmers (and drank tea, natch). <br />
The scene was idyllic - carob, loquat, orange, avocado,<br />
fig, berry, and olive trees, grape vines, fields of<br />
cauliflower, tomatoes, cucumbers, peppers, and<br />
eggplant, lovely little farmers' day huts, and a huge<br />
chicken house.  But the idyll was marred by the fence,<br />
this awful gash that cuts across Palestinian farmers'<br />
lands.  We passed many dried up fields and abandoned<br />
greenhouses belonging to farmers who just can't get<br />
through the security at the gate.  We encountered<br />
numerous road blocks; many dirt roads within this<br />
agricultural area have been rendered impassable by the<br />
Israeli army.  Some of the lands are on the other side<br />
of a settlement bypass road put in during the Oslo<br />
process, and no Palestinian agricultural roads are<br />
allowed to intersect with this Israeli-only West Bank<br />
highway built on confiscated Palestinian land.</p>

<p>Farmer after farmer told us about the assaults on<br />
their livelihood caused by the fence.  One man has a<br />
property that was cut in half by the fence.  He used<br />
to go from one olive grove to the next by walking a<br />
few meters.  Now he has to walk half an hour to the<br />
farmers' gate, and half an hour back.  Another has a<br />
number of farm vehicles at home.  He can get none of<br />
them onto his land.  He has to bring in his crops by<br />
donkey cart, and then unload them by hand onto a<br />
vehicle at the roadblock.  Some farmers have taken to<br />
sleeping in the fields during the week, because the<br />
way home has now been made so circuitous and long.  To<br />
make matters worse, Israel has declared economic war<br />
on Palestinian areas during this Intifada, no longer<br />
allowing Palestinians to export, and using roadblocks<br />
and checkpoints to impede commerce within Palestine. <br />
Qalqilya was once the bread basket of the West Bank,<br />
with exports to Jordan and Iraq as well.  Now, all<br />
produce  goes to market in Qalqilya, at a fraction of<br />
the price.</p>

<p>The attached photo shows me, Andrea from California,<br />
and Eric from Sweden sitting with Mohammad and 3<br />
farmers from Jayyous in an olive grove, talking about<br />
the difficulties of harvesting caused by the fence.</p>

<p>We crossed back through the farmers' gate quickly and<br />
without incident.  There was a security vehicle there,<br />
but no personnel.  We went to the farm of Ziad, also<br />
of the Peasants' Union, for lunch.  He and his son<br />
made a delicious feast for us, cooking everything with<br />
vegetables he picked as he cooked.  While we waited<br />
for lunch to be ready, Mohammad told me about reading<br />
Angela Davis's book about prison in the U.S. while he<br />
was in prison in Israel, and talked about how similar<br />
the conditions are.   He also told me about the little<br />
girl in Qalqilya who's named Angela, after Angela<br />
Davis.</p>

<p>Ziad's farm is breathtaking, but his property, which<br />
used to extend further than it now does, is abruptly<br />
cut off by the fence.  The contrast between the beauty<br />
of well-tended fields of tomatoes and cauliflower, and<br />
rolls of accordion wire blocking entrance to the ditch<br />
in front of the fence, is enough to make one cry.</p>

<p>Back in town, we visited a house that had been visited<br />
during the night by the Israeli army (hence the jeeps<br />
we saw).  There were eleven people in the house: 3<br />
women, one 13-year-old boy, and the rest little girls<br />
(one a baby).  We saw hundreds and hundreds of bullet<br />
holes in the house outside and inside, including in<br />
one of the women's dresses in her closet.  It's a<br />
miracle that no one was shot or killed, and I can't<br />
imagine how frightened the children must have been. <br />
One little girl (I can't say how old she is; I usually<br />
underestimate the age of Palestinian children because<br />
they look so small.  Perhaps it's malnutrition?) was<br />
eager to show us the damage, and they all welcomed the<br />
attention.  The teenage boy lay in a fetal position on<br />
a mat, having had his stomach stomped on by Israeli<br />
border guards in an attempt to force him to say where<br />
they can find the man they were looking for.  They<br />
never found the wanted man, so they took another man<br />
from the family, 26 years old, beat him, and arrested<br />
him.  He may be facing 6 months of administrative<br />
detention now; under Israeli law, no charges have to<br />
be laid for administrative detention to occur.</p>

<p>For the past 2 days our action group has been meeting<br />
with community members about our proposed action at<br />
the Qalqilya wall on Wednesday.  This morning I<br />
participated in meetings at the Palestine People's<br />
Party with someone from the Farmers' Union, and at the<br />
Chamber of Commerce with the Chamber's president.<br />
Everyone has the same story - total economic devastation<br />
as a result of closure and the wall.  The rest of the<br />
day has been preparation for the action - it's a giant<br />
undertaking, but we hope it will be spectacular. <br />
Tomorrow morning I'm off to document the plight of the<br />
villages south of Qalqilya, which have themselves been<br />
encircled by the wall.</p>

<blockquote>
<p><i>Then President Bush's canon will come back to us: "You're either with us or with the terrorists."  Those words hang in time like icicles.  For years to come, butchers and genocidists will fit their grisly mouths around them ("lip-sync," flimmakers call it) to justify their butchery.</i></p>

<p>Arundhati Roy<br />
September, 2002</p>
</blockquote>
</blockquote>

<p>And with that he closes for the night.</p>
