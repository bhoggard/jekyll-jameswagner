<p>[I'm posting the verbatim texts of messages from Steve and three people travelling with him at this time.]</p>

<p>Hi all,</p>

<p>Four of the JAtO [Jews Against the Occupation] affinity group--me, Lisa, Jeremy, and Ryan--are now in Beit Sahour, near Bethlehem.   A fifth member, Erica, is in Askar refugee camp in Nablus, where there has been a lot of shooting and home demolitions. We all are fine. The four of us are being trained here by the International Solidarity Movement tomorrow. We'll then be deployed to whereverwe're needed, and Erica will join us.</p>

<p>All for now.</p>

<p>--Steve</p>

<p>Hey all,<br />
I had a horrific experience at Newark Airport this<br />
morning and thought I should share.  I was tagged<br />
while waiting in line for a direct flight on El-Al and<br />
was pulled aside for a ridiculous series of questions.<br />
 An obnoxious security guard questioned me repeatedly<br />
about my ethnicity, being that I have a South Asian<br />
father and therefore an Indian last name.  He got so<br />
absurd that he actually began asking me if I was sure<br />
if my father was in fact Indian.  At that point I<br />
became visibly flustered and he fed off that.  He then<br />
enrolled me in a Judiasm 101 course where he fired<br />
questions about Judiasm at me for over 10 mintues. <br />
Even though I have been raised Jewish he failed to<br />
believe me and sent me out of the line while everyone<br />
else got to pass on through without problems.  I was<br />
sectioned off and approached by two more guards who<br />
asked the same insane questions and continued to<br />
question the validity of my answers.  I was then,<br />
after an hour, escorted by a security guard into a<br />
special room with all the other "brown" people on the<br />
flight.  All our luggage was dumped out on large<br />
tables and thrown around.  A security guard rifled<br />
through my bras and underwear snickering at the guard<br />
to his left.  My bag was taken out of the room for<br />
over 45 mintues and no one told me what was going on. <br />
The guards laughed at our powerless circumstances<br />
taking pictures with my camera of the other people in<br />
the room "just to make sure the camera was working." <br />
I was taken back behind a curtain and had to undergo a<br />
body search (thank god I was allowed to keep my<br />
clothes on).  When I came out another family was being<br />
taken in.  The kids ran around while their parents had<br />
to undergo the humiliation of the search process. <br />
After almost two and a half hours of waiting I finally<br />
expressed my concern at missing my flight.  Finally a<br />
woman decided to fill the paperwork required for<br />
"someone like me".  Three forms were filled out with<br />
all my info, my luggage was tagged wtih special red,<br />
high security risk, tags, and then I was escorted onto<br />
the plane.  A form with all my information was handed<br />
to the stewardess as I boarded the plane.  She looked<br />
at me with fear and disgust and then let me on the<br />
plane.  I was seated in a special seat.  When the<br />
family next to me saw who I was they conviently moved.<br />
 After almost three hours I was finally in my seat<br />
ready to go. I got treated like shit the entire<br />
flight.</p>

<p>Lisa</p>

<p>I have arrived safely to Palestine. My flight was<br />
uneventful and I had no problems with security.</p>

<p>It feels very strange to be back in Palestine just 14<br />
months after my previous trip. The first thing to<br />
strike me was the amount of new construction occuring<br />
between Ben Gurion airport and Tel Aviv. While I am<br />
sure this is indictative of some political program I<br />
have not been able to give it much thought and thus<br />
have no particular political analysis to offer. Once<br />
in East Jeruslum the obvious less bustle on the<br />
streets is griping. The West Bank also feels very<br />
different with less people on the streets and no<br />
Palestnian security personel to be seen, who a year<br />
ago were very common. Last year, while the Infitfada<br />
was a year old, you still sensed a liveliness on the<br />
streets but today you feel the eerie quiet of<br />
desperation and hopelessness. (My apologies for this<br />
scattered stream of obvervations. My section of future<br />
reports will hopefully be more thought out and<br />
insightful.)</p>

<p>Jeremy</p>

<p>We saw a demolished house today, two blocks away from<br />
the Deheisha Refugee Camp we visited. The camp had<br />
been visited by the Pope on his trip to Bethlehem for<br />
the Bethlehem 2000 celebration, the effects of which<br />
have now evaporated. The house was destroyed by<br />
explosives, and not by bulldozers.  The last remaining<br />
wall, though crooked, held a Palestinian flag, which<br />
rose above the rubble. We were told by a former ISM<br />
organizer, someone now working for the Red Crescent<br />
Society, that the owners of houses scheduled to be<br />
destroyed will fly this flag. On this wall, it was<br />
written in Arabic something to the effect of:  You can<br />
destroy our homes, but you can't destroy our spirit.<br />
She also told us that this very large house was<br />
destroyed because a man who was connected with a<br />
suicide bombing, a man from Jordan, had rented a room<br />
there for two weeks.  This is something of the tour we<br />
had today.</p>

<p>For myself, I have only to say that upon arrival in<br />
Bethlehem I was set upon by a sadness not at all<br />
different from the onset of altitude sickness in our<br />
ascent from the Pacific coast to the upper regions of<br />
Peru in July.</p>

<p>It is terrifying to walk through a town, Bethlehem,<br />
deserted by curfew at 5 in the afternoon, to see the<br />
graffiti of Israeli soldiers, leftover from the April<br />
invasion: Stars of David sprayed on doors and<br />
storefronts, "Israel" labelled in Hebrew to claim the<br />
territory, the name of Rebbe Nachman scrawled and<br />
extended across the entrance to someone's home, Rebbe<br />
Nachman who said that the world is a narrow bridge<br />
whose name appears so wide and distorted.  I remember<br />
in Sarnath, the buddhist pilgrimage town outside of<br />
Benares/Varanasi, how Stars of David and Swastikas<br />
were together posted on the front gates of the Tibetan<br />
institute where I stayed.  I remember how disoriented<br />
I became.  I suffered my first disorientation from the<br />
signs of Jewish power long ago, when I was first<br />
emerging from so many myths.  But, perhaps for the<br />
first time, today I saw this Star of David as having<br />
nothing to do with me.  It was realizing that the star<br />
had nothing to do with me which is what made walking<br />
through the gates at the Tibetan institute bearable. <br />
But is the same realization now, when I am told at the<br />
airport that this star has everything to do with me,<br />
that, once again, the conditions of this occupation<br />
become unbearable. And any sentimental tone I may<br />
convey I now retract.</p>

<p>Ryan.</p>
