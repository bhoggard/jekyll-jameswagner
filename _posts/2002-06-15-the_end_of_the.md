<p>We're <a href="http://www.reuters.com/news_article.jhtml?type=topnews&StoryID=1093750">scared</a> out of our wits, and the Bushies' continual and manipulative <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2002/06/15/opinion/15KELL.html">alarms</a> are doing everything they can to keep the <a href="http://www.thenaturalconnection.net/TNC%20Columns%202000/heebie_jeebies.htm">heebie jeebies </a>going.</p>

<blockquote>
<p>Nine months after the Sept. 11 attacks, the United States finds itself in a jittery mood, as scandal and doubts envelop a growing number of major institutions.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>But there is also sheer fear, and it won't go away.</p>

<blockquote>
<p>During the maximum jitters of the Cuban missile crisis, the high school where I was an impressionable freshman happened to be holding an assembly. The star speaker was a priest from San Francisco, who arranged to have his remarks interrupted by a student delivering a note. The priest studied the note, then looked up with a somber face and announced that the Soviet Union and the United States had just launched nuclear missiles at each other.</p>

<p>Forty years later, I can still hear the terrified whimper in that auditorium as we all considered our imminent doom. But I can't remember a word of what the speaker said afterward. That's the thing about fear: It gets your attention and then refuses to give it back.</p>
</blockquote>
