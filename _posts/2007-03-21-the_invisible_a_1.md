<p>This is a true story (only the names have been withheld, for considerations of privacy and copyright):</p>

<blockquote>
<p>A young artist is chosen to be in a group show at a respectable small non-profit space.</p>

<p>An appreciative and enthusiastic art blogger captures an image of the artist's work installed in that space and publishes it on his site.</p>

<p>On a return visit to the space months later the blogger is told by people in charge that photographs are not allowed at any time.</p>

<p>The blogger ceases to photograph any artists' work in that space.</p>

<p>Two years after the image of the young artist's work appeared on the blogger's site a major museum in another city writes to him asking if it could have permission to use it in publicity materials being prepared prior to a solo show it has scheduled of the artist's work, since there is no other photograph of the piece available.</p>

<p>The blogger suspects that the piece itself may no longer physically exist, thus explaining the importance of his photograph.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>What does the blogger do in this case, and in the larger scheme of things, what does this scenario say about our cultural institutions' photography restrictions generally?</p>
