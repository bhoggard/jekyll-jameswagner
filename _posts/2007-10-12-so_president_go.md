<p><img alt="PureGore.jpg" src="/mt_archives/PureGore.jpg" width="266" height="384" /><br />
the once and future president</p>

<p>I'm not arguing he should be nominated and elected this time because <a href="http://www.guardian.co.uk/environment/2007/oct/12/climatechange.internationalnews">he won the Nobel Peace Prize</a>, but because winning the Nobel Peace Prize can make it happen.</p>

<p>Why Al Gore, and not anyone from within the current field of designed and positioned candidates?   To begin, because I can't support much of what I'm hearing from any of the three current "frontrunners";  to continue, because I believe Gore says what he thinks, not what he thinks others think he should say;  and to conclude, because he would be elected.</p>

<p>Although I can't know what was going on in his mind at the time, I realize that I might have to advance one doubt about Gore's reputation for plain speaking:  Had the man we voted for once before* been candid and upright about the truth in November and December of 2000 the world would be a much better place today, and we would now be thinking about who should succeed a President Gore.</p>

<p>*<br />
Although not "we", since as a New Yorker I could pull the handle for Nader without affecting the Electoral College votes.</p>

<p>ps:  I'm also pretty happy with what Richardson is saying, I've always thought Kucinich had it right on just about everything, and Mike Gravel should be getting more of a hearing.  I'm still really disturbed about the Democratic Party as it now exists, and while I don't know how or whether I could reconcile that with a Gore, Richardson, Kucinich or Gravel candidacy, it could be very exciting finding out.</p>
