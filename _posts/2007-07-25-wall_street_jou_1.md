<p>I guess none of our fans read the <u>Wall</u> <u>Street</u> <u>Journal</u> (I suppose that's quite possible), because no one told us until today about <a href="http://online.wsj.com/article/SB118496967641073476.html">this item</a> by Lauren A. E. Schuker from last Saturday's edition.  It's all about telling readers where they might find the next art bargains, and among a number of other ideas are the writer's suggestions for checking out the web.</p>

<blockquote>
<p>Art blogs can also be a good source of information about emerging artists. Popular sites include <a href="http://www.artcal.net/">artcal.net</a>, <a href="http://www.artnet.com/">artnet.com</a>, <a href="http://edwardwinkleman.blogspot.com/">edwardwinkleman.blogspot.com</a>, <a href="http://galleryhopper.org/">galleryhopper.org</a> and <a href="http://www.artsjournal.com/man/">artsjournal.com/man</a>.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>I can't help noticing that ArtCal was placed before artnet, and this isn't an alphabetical list.</p>

<p>Maybe <u>WSJ</u> fans will now be tuning in regularly.  In any event, the Schuker's piece seems like a good thing for everybody.  It doesn't seem it would hurt if our money moguls got more culture and it would definitely help underknown artists and galleries if people with money stopped tripping over each other chasing down the safest art-world stars.</p>

<p>Meanwhile, an inside tip from <a href="http://bloggy.com/">bloggy</a> and <a href="https://jameswagner.com/">jameswagner</a>:  ArtCal will be launching a totally new design in the next week or two.  The trim and stylish new logo appears at the top of this post.</p>
