I'm thinking a blood-red ink would be more appropriate about now</p>

<p>Well of course he and Bush <a href="http://news.bbc.co.uk/2/hi/asia-pacific/7048284.stm">get along</a>.   Although he was presented the Nobel Peace Prize in 1989 "for his consistent resistance to the use of violence," the Dalai Lama (aka Tenzin Gyatso) supports war, including the Iraq war.    Of course by now we know that the Dali Lama supports <em>everything</em>.  It's what makes him so popular.</p>

<p>Today I accidentally came across a <a href="/2003/09/dalai_lama_war.html">post</a> I wrote four years ago at about the time of the last visit to the White House of the West's vagabond Tibetan saint obsession [what I call the bandwagon/group-think syndrome].  I think it was already the smiling Gyatso's second audience with Bush, and at that time I excerpted a report on the <u>Guardian</u> site:</p>

<blockquote>
<p>The Dalai Lama said Wednesday that the U.S.-led war in Afghanistan may have been justified to win a larger peace, but that is it too soon to judge whether the Iraq war was warranted.</p>

<p>"I think history will tell," he said in an interview with The Associated Press on Wednesday, just after he met with President Bush.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>I've looked everywhere online, but I couldn't find any sign that history has told His vacuous Holiness anything yet.</p>

<p>I'll end by confessing that I'm no longer capable of being surprised by another form of hypocrisy, that of our peace-lovers in Congress pretending to be <a href="http://voanews.com/english/2007-10-17-voa45.cfm">fired up</a> about a Chinese-occupied Tibet while they continue to pursue the ruination and occupation of their own, equally-defenseless victim state in the Middle East.</p>
