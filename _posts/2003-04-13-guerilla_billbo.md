<p><a href="http://www.popaganda.com/">Ron English</a> borrows <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2003/04/13/nyregion/13BILL.html?pagewanted=1">billboards</a> to advertise his politics.</p>

<blockquote>
<p>Ron English puts up illegal billboards, so he has only one way of knowing if it has been a good day.</p>

<p>"I consider it a success if I don't go to jail," he explained. He should know. He has had two very unsuccessful days in the past.</p>

<p>You may have seen Mr. English, a 43-year-old father of two, wandering around the streets of Manhattan or New Jersey with a bucket of glue, a set of rollers and a crew of accomplices. He plasters his original paintings in broad daylight on billboards he does not own. This is a conscious decision, because billboarding in the dark would only look more suspicious. "If you're out at night," he said, "it's obvious that you're not supposed to be there."</p>

<p>. . . .</p>

<p>"Ron's kind of a one-man billboard hurricane," said Jack Napier, the founder of the <a href="http://www.billboardliberation.com/">Billboard Liberation Front</a>, a San Francisco-based movement considered one of the first to alter such advertising. "He's done some brilliant stuff."</p>

<p>Two weeks ago, Mr. English pasted up three works in Jersey City, where he lives and paints. One reads: "Saddam's SUV's. Oil Dependence Day Sale." It ends with the Chevy logo and the tag "Like Iraq."</p>
</blockquote>

<p>He frankly admits, in his own words, "I guess I'm a criminal.  But I don't think I'm a nuisance to society."</p>
